## BLOCK 1 — OPENER + HOMEWORK REVIEW (~20 min) — SIT

Open seated. Before the student arrives, write on the corner of the board the Phrase of the Day, the words **FORTALEZA day 2/3**, and the heading **HOMEWORK CHECK — 3 PARTS**. Nothing else. Everything else generates live.

Welcome her. Brief check on how she felt after session 1 — listen for 30 seconds, no more. If she says it was hard: *"Of course it was. That's the work. Today it gets a little easier because you already know what you're listening for."* If she says it was fine: *"Then today we push."* Either way, move quickly into the Phrase.

Land the Phrase of the Day. Point to the board, read once, she echoes twice, then solo from memory. Gloss it: *"Yesterday was day one — we named what is hard. Today is day two — we build. Strength is not the first time you do a thing; strength is the second time you do the same thing when you already know it's hard. The reps are what build the muscle. We're in the reps today."* **Phrase of the Day INSERTION #1.**

```
PHRASE OF THE DAY — SESION 2
"Fortitude builds — strength is doing the rep
 one more time when the body already wants
 to stop."
```

Frame FORTALEZA day 2: *"Day one we named the three weak points. Day two — today — we work them. Day three we simulate the reexamen. Today is the heaviest day because we do question forms AND a full dictation. Pace yourself."* Brief, no moralizing.

Now homework. *"Show me what you brought. Three parts — let's see them."*

```
HOMEWORK CHECK — 3 PARTS (Sesion 1 -> Sesion 2)

  (1) LISTENING: 5 sentences from a YouTube clip
      -> she shows the paper, reads 2 aloud
      -> tutor notes: did she catch contractions? prepositions?
                      past endings?

  (2) WRITING: 10 minimal-pair sentences
      -> tutor reads 3 of them aloud at random;
         she has to TELL THE TUTOR which word she meant
         (e.g. ship or sheep?)
      -> tests both her writing AND her pronunciation

  (3) RECORDING: audio sent to coordination WhatsApp
      -> tutor confirms it arrived (coordination tracks)
      -> tutor does NOT play it back in session;
         just confirms delivery
```

For each part, take 3-4 minutes. Use the paper she brought as evidence of where the same 3 weak points from session 1 either improved or stayed stuck. Note this in your tutor's notebook with her name and literal examples — this is what coordination needs to see for progress tracking.

**PLAN B — homework not done:** if she arrives with nothing, do NOT scold, do NOT moralize, do NOT spend session time guilt-tripping. Move directly to a **5-minute spoken diagnostic** instead — you ask her 8 oral A1 questions at normal speed (mix of yes/no + wh-questions) and you note in your notebook: did she UNDERSTAND the question? (separate from whether she answered correctly). The diagnostic gives you the same data the homework would have given — slightly less, but enough. The session does not collapse because of missing homework. Move to block 2 at the 20-min mark.

```
PLAN B — 5-MIN SPOKEN DIAGNOSTIC (homework not done)

   8 questions at normal speed, no repeat.
   Tutor checks "understood?" YES/NO in notebook.

    1. What's your name?
    2. Where do you live?
    3. Do you work or do you study?
    4. What time did you wake up this morning?
    5. Can you cook?
    6. Were you at home last weekend?
    7. What will you do tomorrow?
    8. Have you ever traveled outside Colombia?
```

The moment you've taken stock — homework reviewed OR Plan B diagnostic done — move on. Do not let block 1 leak past 22 minutes; the content is the priority.

⚠ **ANCHOR (safety):** if the student arrives visibly upset (anxiety about the reexamen, family crisis, sleep problems), name it ONCE: *"You don't look great today. Anything I should know before we start?"* Listen for ONE minute, no more. Then: *"Got it. We work anyway. The reexamen doesn't move because of a bad week."* Pivot to the Phrase and the homework with no further detour. Tutoring is not therapy — the student needs to feel heard once and then moved.

## BLOCK 2 — CONTENT — A1 QUESTION FORMS (~50 min) — STAND for the open, SIT for the drill

Open standing at the board. Erase everything except the Phrase. *"In A1 listening, half of what you miss is QUESTIONS. The reexamen will ask questions. The audio will contain questions. If you don't catch the question structure, you don't catch the meaning. Today we make question forms automatic — so when you hear them, your brain says 'that's a yes-no' or 'that's a wh' before the sentence even finishes."*

Write the 5 question types on the board as you say them aloud. Student copies into her notebook (the writing-it-herself is part of the drill — 2 minutes, do not skip):

```
A1 QUESTION FORMS — 5 TYPES (student copies into her notebook)

TYPE 1 — YES/NO with DO/DOES (present simple)
   "Do you work?"       "Does she live here?"
   structure:           DO/DOES + subject + base verb ?
   short answer:        "Yes, I do." / "No, she doesn't."

TYPE 2 — WH-QUESTION with DO/DOES (present simple)
   "Where do you work?"  "What does she study?"
   structure:           WH + DO/DOES + subject + base verb ?
   short answer:        a noun or short phrase, not "yes/no"

TYPE 3 — YES/NO + WH with DID (past simple)
   "Did you go?"        "When did you arrive?"
   structure:           (WH) + DID + subject + base verb ?
   common trap:         DID kills the past on the verb
                        ("Did you went" is WRONG -> "Did you go")

TYPE 4 — TO BE QUESTIONS (am/is/are/was/were)
   "Are you tired?"     "Was she at home?"   "Where were you?"
   structure:           BE-verb + subject + complement ?
   no DO/DOES with BE

TYPE 5 — CAN/COULD/WILL/SHOULD QUESTIONS
   "Can you swim?"      "Will you call me?"  "Should I wait?"
   structure:           MODAL + subject + base verb ?
   short answer:        "Yes, I can." / "No, I won't."
```

Pronounce-then-drill. For each type, you say 2 example questions at normal speed (don't slow down — she has to learn to hear them as they actually sound). She listens, then she answers each one orally with a complete short answer. After all 5 types are pronounced + answered, you check her notebook copy quickly — circle any structural error she copied. **Phrase of the Day INSERTION #2:** *"This is the rep — the second time. Doing it again, more cleanly. Strength."*

Now the dictation. *"20 questions, in batches of 5. I say each question once at normal speed. You answer verbally — short answer, full sentence. If you don't catch the question, say 'no entiendo' — I repeat once. Then we move."*

```
QUESTION DICTATION — 20 QUESTIONS IN 4 BATCHES OF 5

BATCH 1 — yes/no with DO/DOES
   1. Do you live in Bogota?
   2. Does your mother work?
   3. Do you have brothers?
   4. Does it rain a lot here?
   5. Do you like spicy food?

BATCH 2 — wh-questions with DO/DOES
   6. Where do you work?
   7. What does your father do?
   8. When do you study English?
   9. Why do you want to learn English?
  10. How do you go to work?

BATCH 3 — past with DID and TO BE past
  11. Did you eat breakfast?
  12. Where did you go yesterday?
  13. Was your weekend good?
  14. Were you born in Colombia?
  15. What time did you arrive today?

BATCH 4 — modals and TO BE present
  16. Can you cook arepas?
  17. Will you study tonight?
  18. Should I speak slower?
  19. Are you tired now?
  20. Is your sister older than you?
```

After each batch of 5, stop. Together, on the board, write the THREE most common errors she made in that batch (anonymous — names in your notebook). Could be: she missed the auxiliary, she answered with the wrong subject, she used DID with a past-form verb, she confused IS and ARE. Whatever it is — make it visible, make her echo the correction, then move to the next batch.

```
ERROR RECAP PATTERN (after each batch, write on board, she echoes):
   X "_____________"   ->  "_____________"   (why it failed)
   X "_____________"   ->  "_____________"
   X "_____________"   ->  "_____________"
```

By batch 3, she should be catching her own errors before you write them. By batch 4, she should be predicting the question type from the first word. That's the muscle.

*Plan B: if she's drowning by batch 2 (you've had to repeat more than half the questions), do NOT push through to batch 4. Land batches 1 and 2 cleanly, then jump to ONE batch of mixed types (5 questions of your choice covering the gaps you saw). 10 well-handled questions beat 20 chaotic ones.*

**Phrase of the Day INSERTION #3** at the transition between batch 3 and batch 4: *"Body wants to stop. That's the moment. One more batch. Strength."*

Close the block with a 2-minute lightning round — you ask 5 quick questions, mixing types randomly, no pause between, she answers in short-answer form only ("Yes, I do." / "No, I don't." / "At seven." / "In Cali."). This is to compress the response time. The reexamen will not wait. *"You are ready for block 3 — now we listen to a paragraph and we write it down."*

⚠ **ANCHOR (safety):** if 35 minutes into the block she still confuses DO/DOES vs DID (treating them as interchangeable), do NOT advance to batches 3 and 4. Stay in present simple (batches 1 and 2 + their mixed variations) and land THAT cleanly. The past with DID can land in session 3 alongside the simulation. Better solid present than shaky past + shaky present.

## BLOCK 3 — LIVE APPLICATION — DICTADO DIRIGIDO (~40 min) — SIT, book closed for student

The structure is fixed and you set it up clearly:

```
DICTADO DIRIGIDO — 2-3 ONE-MINUTE A1 NARRATIVES

  STEP 1: Tutor reads the WHOLE narrative at normal speed.
          Student listens. No writing. ~1 minute.
  STEP 2: Tutor reads the SAME narrative again at normal speed.
          Student transcribes everything she catches.
          Dashes for missed words. ~1 minute + ~3 min writing.
  STEP 3: Word by word — tutor reads ONE sentence at a time,
          student compares what she wrote, corrects in her notebook
          in a different color (or with a clear mark).
  STEP 4: Together identify which TYPE of error dominated this
          narrative: contractions? past -ed? prepositions?
          weak forms? auxiliaries?
  STEP 5: Move to next narrative.

  Each narrative cycle = ~12-13 minutes.
  Target: 2 narratives minimum, 3 if time allows.
```

Pick the narratives from the A1 book (any 1-minute prose chunk from a module she's already covered — vocabulary sections work well, as do the question/answer examples). The first narrative should be Module 1-7 territory (present simple, can, basic). The second should be Module 8-15 (past simple introduced). The third (if time allows) should be Module 16-20 (future or modals).

For each narrative cycle, follow the 5 steps EXACTLY. The temptation is to repeat the audio a third time or to slow down — DO NOT. The reexamen reads twice, normal speed. The drill must match the reexamen. *"This is the same condition as the real test. Twice. Normal speed. That's it."*

The most important part is STEP 3 — going word by word together. This is where the listening map gets built. As she sees the gap between what she wrote and what was actually there, she learns to predict WHAT she misses. Pattern-spot openly with her: *"Look — in narrative 1 you missed 'doesn't' three times. In narrative 2 you missed it twice more. That's a pattern. Tomorrow in the simulation you will hear contractions — you'll know to listen for them."*

Note in your tutor's notebook (with name) the literal errors per narrative and the dominant pattern. Compare against session 1's three weak points. If the same pattern is still dominant → it goes to session 3 as the main target. If a new pattern emerged → add it to the watch list.

*Plan B: if she's missing more than 40% of each narrative on transcription, the narratives are too long for her current state. SHORTEN: for narrative 2 onwards, read only HALF the narrative (~30 seconds, ~50 words). The structure stays — read twice, transcribe, correct word-by-word, name the pattern. Shrinking the input keeps the drill intact.*

**Phrase of the Day INSERTION #4** between narrative 1 and narrative 2, while she's resting her hand from writing: *"You are doing the rep. Body wants to stop. We don't stop. One more narrative."*

Close the block: *"You just did dictation. The reexamen doesn't do full dictation, but if you can transcribe a narrative, you can answer comprehension questions about it. The skill is the same — staying with the audio until the meaning lands. Block 4 closes the session."*

⚠ **ANCHOR (safety):** if she's exhausted after narrative 2 (head down, eyes glazed, hand cramping from writing), STOP at narrative 2 and move to block 4. Do not push for narrative 3. The dictation is the heaviest drill in the whole 6-hour arc — fatigue is real and expected. Two solid narratives + clear pattern identification beats three rushed narratives. End on the second narrative's corrected transcription, with the pattern named.

## BLOCK 4 — CLOSE + HOMEWORK (~10 min) — SIT

Sit her down, calm. The session was heavy. Let the room settle for 5 seconds before you speak.

*"Three things to close. Same shape as yesterday."*

**Thing 1: 3 specific gaps from today.** Walk to the board. Write the 3 dominant patterns from blocks 2 and 3 (anonymous on board; names in tutor's notebook). These may overlap with session 1's three weak points — if so, that's confirmation; if not, the watch list is growing. Read each out loud, she echoes.

```
GAPS — SESION 2 RECAP (anonymous on board; details in tutor's notebook)
 1. _____________ (e.g. auxiliary confusion — DO vs DOES vs DID)
 2. _____________ (e.g. past -ed disappearing on second listen)
 3. _____________ (e.g. weak-form prepositions missed in dictation)
```

**Thing 2: Phrase close.** She says the Phrase of the Day from memory. Then she paraphrases it in her own words — any honest paraphrase that holds the *one more rep + strength when the body wants to stop* idea. Target shape: *"I keep going even when I'm tired."* Accept it.

**Thing 3: homework.** Write it on the board while you explain, she copies into her notebook:

```
HOMEWORK SESION 2 — DUE BEFORE SESION 3 (within 48h)
TIME: 20-30 min total (A1 level). NOT fragmented.

(1) LISTENING + TRANSCRIPTION (15 min) — find ONE short
    A1 dialogue on YouTube (search: "easy English
    dialogue beginner" or "elementary English
    conversation"). Listen ONCE without subtitles,
    then ONCE with subtitles OFF and TRANSCRIBE
    the first 30 seconds in your notebook. Compare
    with subtitles ON after. Note 3 things you
    missed. Bring on paper.

(2) QUESTION FORMS (10 min) — write 10 questions
    using the 5 types from block 2 (2 per type).
    Examples:
       Type 1: "Do you like rice?"
       Type 4: "Were you born in 1990?"
    Bring on paper.

(3) RECORDING (5 min) — record yourself ASKING
    those 10 questions aloud, with natural rhythm
    and rising/falling intonation. Send to coordination
    WhatsApp before session 3. Label: A1-REEX-2.

RULE: not fragmented. Same Plan B as last time —
if homework not done, session 3 opens with a 5-min
diagnostic instead of review. The work belongs to you.
```

Plan B if no smartphone/YouTube/WhatsApp: task 2 (question writing) is enough alone. Document it in tutor's notebook.

Brief Session 3 announce: *"Session 3 — full mock A1 Final, debrief, exam-day strategy. Bring your notebook, this homework on paper, a pen you trust, and water. The mock takes most of the session. Sleep well."* No more details.

Close: *"Today was day TWO of FORTALEZA. You built the muscle. Day three we simulate. See you session 3."* Two-second pause. *"Coordination handles results. The six hours are mine — what you do with them is yours."*

⚠ **ANCHOR (safety):** if block 4 runs short because the dictation went long, the priority order is identical to session 1: (1) Phrase close MUST happen — she says it from memory; (2) homework MUST be written on the board and copied — Plan B documented; (3) the three gaps recap can compress to ONE sentence; (4) Session 3 announce can compress to ONE sentence. Never cut homework. Never cut Phrase close.
