**Date:** ___/___/2026 · **Attending:** 1 student (1-on-1 tutoring)
**Reexamen context:** Student failed A1 Final with 60%. Weakest area declared by coordination: **LISTENING**. Reexamen window is open — the tutor is not the examiner.
**Virtue:** FORTALEZA day 1 of 3 — TODAY OPENS the cycle of three tutoring sessions. Day 1 = naming what is hard without flinching.
**Phrase of the Day:** *"Fortitude listens first — strength is staying in the sound even when the meaning has not arrived."*

## BLOCK 1 — OPENER + DIAGNOSTIC (~25 min) — SIT facing the board

Open seated, calm. Before the student enters, write on the corner of the board the Phrase of the Day, the word **FORTALEZA day 1/3**, and the heading **DICTATION DIAGNOSTIC — 10 SENTENCES**. Nothing else. Everything that comes next gets generated live.

Welcome the student and name where she is: this is Session 1 of 3 tutoring sessions, two hours each, six hours total, leading into her A1 reexamen. No judgment, no pity. *"You took the Final. You scored 60%. Coordination handles results — not me. What I have is six hours with you, and we use them to make sure listening stops being the thing that costs you points. That's the whole job."* If she tries to explain why she failed, listen for thirty seconds, then redirect: *"I hear you. We move."*

Land the Phrase of the Day. Point to the board, read it once slowly, student echoes twice, then she says it solo from memory. Gloss it briefly: *"Fortitude is strength under pressure. Listening is pressure — sounds arrive fast and meaning arrives late. The strength is to stay in the sound even when you do not understand yet. Don't translate. Don't close your eyes in frustration. Stay."* **Phrase of the Day INSERTION #1.**

```
PHRASE OF THE DAY — SESION 1
"Fortitude listens first — strength is staying
 in the sound even when the meaning has not
 arrived."
```

Frame FORTALEZA day 1: *"Three sessions, one virtue. Today is day ONE — the OPENING. Today we name what is hard. Day 2 we build the muscle. Day 3 we run the simulation. That's the arc. FORTALEZA all three days because the reexamen needs nerve, not luck."* Name it, anchor it, move.

Now the diagnostic — this is the central work of block 1. *"I'm going to dictate 10 sentences. They start easy and they get harder. You write every word you hear in your notebook. If you don't catch a word, write a dash. Don't ask me to repeat — I repeat each sentence exactly twice and then we move. At the end, we look at the page together and we see exactly where listening breaks for you."*

Dictate the 10 sentences slowly the first time, normal speed the second. Build them as you go — they don't need to be printed because you generate them aloud. Suggested arc (tutor adapts to the student's apparent level — these are the floor, raise if she lands them cleanly):

```
DICTATION DIAGNOSTIC — 10 SENTENCES (tutor dictates, student writes)

 1. My name is Ana and I am from Colombia.
 2. He works at a hospital on Mondays.
 3. She doesn't like coffee, but she likes tea.
 4. I can speak a little English.
 5. What time do you start work?
 6. They didn't go to the party last weekend.
 7. There are three apples on the table.
 8. I won't be at home tomorrow.
 9. Where were you yesterday at six o'clock?
10. He's been waiting for the bus for twenty minutes.

REPEAT EACH SENTENCE EXACTLY TWICE. NORMAL SPEED.
DO NOT SLOW DOWN. DO NOT SPELL.
```

*Plan B: if the student gets visibly overwhelmed by sentence 5 and her notebook is blank, stop the dictation, take a breath, and restart from sentence 1 — but this time she only writes the FIRST and LAST word of each sentence. Even getting first + last anchors the listening muscle. Do NOT skip the diagnostic — it is the map for sessions 2 and 3.*

When the 10 sentences are dictated, sit beside her and read the page together. Mark with a circle every place she missed a word, swapped a sound, or wrote a dash. **Do NOT correct her writing yet** — first you read the page back to her exactly as she wrote it (mistakes and all), and you ask her: *"What do you hear differently now?"* She often catches her own errors when she sees them written. Then together you write the correct version under hers, and you circle the THREE patterns that repeat most (e.g. she missed contractions, she missed past simple endings, she missed prepositions). Those three patterns are the spine of blocks 2 and 3.

Write the three patterns on the board as a small heading:

```
WEAK POINTS — SESION 1 DIAGNOSTIC (student's own)
 1. _________________________ (e.g. contractions: doesn't, won't, he's)
 2. _________________________ (e.g. past simple -ed endings: worked, waited)
 3. _________________________ (e.g. prepositions: on, at, for)
```

The moment the student can name back to you her own three weak points without reading the board, move on. *"Now we work on those. Block 2."*

⚠ **ANCHOR (safety):** if the diagnostic is going longer than 22 minutes (you're still on sentence 6, the student is frustrated, or you're spending too much time reading the page together), STOP at 22 minutes and call it. Take whatever data you have — even 6 sentences is enough to find two patterns. The content blocks 2 and 3 cannot be sacrificed for a perfect diagnostic. Better to land block 2 well with rough diagnostic data than to have a perfect map and no time to work.

## BLOCK 2 — CONTENT — CRITICAL A1 SOUNDS + CONNECTED SPEECH (~50 min) — STAND for the open, SIT for the drill

Open standing at the board. Erase everything except the Phrase of the Day and the three weak points from block 1. *"Listening fails for A1 Spanish speakers in two places: SOUNDS that don't exist in Spanish, and CONNECTED SPEECH — the way English glues words together. We work both. First sounds."*

Write the 6 critical A1 phonemes on the board as you say them aloud. Build minimal pairs LIVE — no printed list. The student copies into her notebook as you write (the act of writing them herself is part of the drill — takes about 3 minutes, do not skip):

```
CRITICAL A1 SOUNDS — 6 PHONEMES SPANISH MISSES
(write on board, student copies into her notebook)

1. /iː/ vs /ɪ/      sheep / ship       feet / fit       beach / bitch
2. /b/ vs /v/       berry / very       boat / vote      ban / van
3. /θ/ vs /s/       think / sink       thing / sing     bath / bass
4. /ʃ/ vs /tʃ/      ship / chip        share / chair    wash / watch
5. /h/ aspirated    hat / at           hi / I           heart / art
6. /ə/ schwa        the (thuh)         banana (buh-NA-nuh)   about (uh-BOUT)
```

Now the drill. Stand at the board and point to pair 1. Pronounce each word twice — exaggerated first, normal second. Student repeats both. Then you say ONE of the two words (your choice — keep it random) and she points to the word on the board OR says "1" or "2". Run through all 6 pairs this way, both directions (you say it, she points; she says it, you point — yes, she also produces). After one full pass, do a discrimination dictation: you say 8 random single words from the 6 pairs in any order, she writes only the number (1 or 2) of which she heard. Read her answers back, mark which she missed, repeat the missed ones twice each. **Phrase of the Day INSERTION #2:** *"Strength is staying in the sound — even when the meaning has not arrived. Right now there is no meaning, only the sound. Stay."*

The moment she's hitting at least 6/8 on the discrimination dictation across two passes, move to connected speech. Sit her down. *"English does not say each word separately. It glues words together. Three rules — write them with me."*

```
CONNECTED SPEECH — 3 RULES THAT BREAK A1 LISTENING

RULE 1 — LINKING (consonant + vowel)
   "an apple"            sounds like    "a-napple"
   "turn it on"          sounds like    "tur-ni-ton"
   "look at it"          sounds like    "loo-ka-tit"

RULE 2 — CONTRACTIONS (the missing letters)
   "I am" -> I'm         "you are" -> you're      "he is" -> he's
   "do not" -> don't     "does not" -> doesn't    "will not" -> won't
   "I would" -> I'd      "I have" -> I've         "she has" -> she's
   The contraction is ALMOST INVISIBLE in speech. That's why you miss it.

RULE 3 — WEAK FORMS (the small words shrink)
   "to" sounds like "tuh"       "I want to go" -> "I wanna go" (informal)
   "and" sounds like "n"         "fish and chips" -> "fish-n-chips"
   "for" sounds like "fer"       "for me" -> "fer me"
   "of" sounds like "uv"         "cup of coffee" -> "cup-uv-coffee"
   "a" sounds like "uh"          "a book" -> "uh-book"
```

Now you dictate 10 sentences that use heavy linking + contractions + weak forms — the same way you did the diagnostic, twice each at normal speed. She writes. Then together you decode: she circles every place a contraction or linking caught her. Examples to dictate (build live, do not read from any printout):

```
CONNECTED SPEECH DICTATION — 10 SENTENCES

 1. I'm going to the store.            (I'm + going-tuh)
 2. She doesn't want a coffee.          (doesn't + wuhn-tuh)
 3. He's at the office.                  (he's + at-thuh)
 4. We'll see you tomorrow.              (we'll + see-yuh)
 5. They don't have any money.           (don't + have-ny)
 6. I'd like a cup of tea.               (I'd + cup-uv)
 7. What time does it start?             (does-it + sounds like dzit)
 8. I can't hear you.                    (can't + hear-yuh)
 9. There's a man at the door.           (there's + at-thuh)
10. Where are you from?                  (where-yuh + from)
```

Decode each one together after she writes them. Show her — on the board — where the linking happens (draw a curved line under the linked syllables). Show her where the contraction lives (circle it). Show her where the weak form lives (underline it). **Do not lecture — show, circle, ask her to point at it.** This is the muscle.

*Plan B: if the student is exhausted by sentence 6 of the connected-speech dictation, stop and decode the 6 she has. Six well-decoded sentences beat ten rushed sentences. The pattern is what matters, not the volume.*

Close the block by going back to the three weak points she named in block 1. Ask her: *"Of the three things you missed in the diagnostic, which two showed up again here?"* She'll name them. **Phrase of the Day INSERTION #3:** *"Fortitude is naming what is hard so you can work on it — not pretending it's easy."* Then stand. *"Block 3 — we take what we just worked on and we listen to real A1 text from the book."*

⚠ **ANCHOR (safety):** if 35 minutes into the block she still confuses /b/ vs /v/ AND /θ/ vs /s/ AND misses every contraction, do NOT load all 3 connected-speech rules. Pick the ONE rule that maps to her top weak point from block 1 (if she missed contractions in the diagnostic — rule 2; if she missed prepositions — rule 3 weak forms; if she missed verb endings — rule 1 linking). Land ONE rule well. The other two can land in session 2. Better one rule mastered than three rules confused.

## BLOCK 3 — LIVE APPLICATION — A1 BOOK LISTENING DRILL (~35 min) — SIT, book closed for student

Open the printed A1 book on your lap. Student sits opposite you with a closed notebook and a pen. *"Now we listen to real A1 text. You don't have the book. I read. You listen. Twice each time. Then questions."*

The drill structure is the same for every paragraph and you set it up once, clearly:

```
LISTENING DRILL — 4 PARAGRAPHS, SAME STRUCTURE EACH

  STEP 1: Tutor reads paragraph at NORMAL speed. Student listens.
          No writing.
  STEP 2: Tutor reads SAME paragraph again. Student listens.
          No writing.
  STEP 3: Tutor asks 3 comprehension questions. Student answers verbally.
  STEP 4: Tutor reads paragraph a THIRD time. Student listens
          ONLY for one specific element (number, name, time, place).
  STEP 5: Student reports the element. Tutor confirms or corrects.

  No translation. No "I didn't catch it." She answers what she heard.
```

Pick 4 paragraphs from the A1 book of increasing difficulty. Suggested anchors (tutor uses these if the book is open to a usable page; if not, picks the equivalent at the appropriate level):

- **Paragraph 1 (warm-up):** a short Module 1-3 paragraph — present simple, can, basic vocabulary, 3-4 sentences. *Questions: who, what, where.* *Specific element: a NUMBER (an age, a quantity).*
- **Paragraph 2 (build):** a Module 4-7 paragraph — present simple negative, do/does questions, 4-5 sentences. *Questions: what does X do, where does X live, what doesn't X like.* *Specific element: a TIME (an hour, a day).*
- **Paragraph 3 (push):** a Module 8-12 paragraph with past simple — regular and irregular verbs, 5-6 sentences. *Questions: what happened, when, why.* *Specific element: a NAME (a person, a place).*
- **Paragraph 4 (stretch):** a Module 13-18 paragraph with future or modal — will, can, should, 6-7 sentences. *Questions: what will X do, what should X do, what can X do.* *Specific element: a PREPOSITION + day or time.*

For each paragraph, follow the 5-step structure exactly. Do not deviate. Do not let her ask you to repeat outside the structure — that's the rule of the drill, and the rule itself is part of the practice for the reexamen (where she will get audio twice and no more).

After paragraph 2 — **Phrase of the Day INSERTION #4:** *"You are staying in the sound. The meaning is arriving. That is fortitude — strength is what happens when you don't quit at the first confusion."* Brief, then keep moving.

Between paragraphs, note in your tutor's notebook (with her name, this is the personal-notebook protocol for 1-on-1) the specific phrases or words she missed each time. Pattern-spot: is it contractions again? Is it past -ed? Is it prepositions? The pattern in block 3 should echo the pattern from blocks 1 and 2 — if it does, that's confirmation; if it shifts, that's new data for session 2.

*Plan B: if a paragraph is too long for her current state (you can see her glazing over), CUT THE LAST SENTENCE before you read it the second time. Use only the part you read the first time. The structure stays — read twice, ask, re-listen for one element. Shrinking a paragraph beats abandoning the drill.*

After paragraph 4 (or after 3 if you ran out of time — that's fine, 3 paragraphs done well beats 4 rushed), close the block. *"You just did the exact thing the reexamen asks you to do. The reexamen reads audio twice. You answer. That's it. You are not blind to it anymore. Block 4 closes the session."*

⚠ **ANCHOR (safety):** if the student is visibly tired or shutting down by paragraph 3, STOP at paragraph 3 and move to block 4. Do not push for paragraph 4. The goal of block 3 is to build confidence in the listening structure, not to exhaust her. A tired listener at the end of session 1 will dread session 2. End block 3 with a win, even a small one.

## BLOCK 4 — CLOSE + HOMEWORK (~10 min) — SIT

Sit her down, calm. *"We close. Three things."*

**Thing 1: the three weak points from today.** Walk to the board. The three weak points from block 1 are already written. Under each, write ONE concrete word that came up today as evidence of that weakness. Read each one out loud and have her echo. *"Those three are what you carry into session 2. Anonymous on the board, but I write them in my notebook with details — that's how coordination tracks your progress."*

```
WEAK POINTS — SESION 1 RECAP (anonymous on board; details in tutor's notebook)
 1. _____________ (e.g. contractions — missed "doesn't", "he's", "won't")
 2. _____________ (e.g. past -ed endings — missed "worked", "waited")
 3. _____________ (e.g. prepositions — missed "on", "at", "for")
```

**Thing 2: Phrase close.** She says the Phrase of the Day from memory. Then she paraphrases it in her own words — any honest paraphrase that holds the *staying in the sound + strength* idea. Target shape: *"I keep listening even when I don't understand yet."* Accept it, do not over-correct.

**Thing 3: homework.** Write it on the board while you explain it out loud. The student copies into her notebook so it goes home with her:

```
HOMEWORK SESION 1 — DUE BEFORE SESION 2 (within 48h)
TIME: 20-30 min total (A1 level). NOT fragmented.

(1) LISTENING (10 min) — find ONE short YouTube clip in
    English with subtitles OFF. Search "easy English
    conversation A1" or "elementary English listening".
    Listen TWICE without subtitles. Write 5 sentences
    you heard (or your best guess of what you heard).
    Bring on paper to session 2.

(2) WRITING (10 min) — write 10 sentences using the
    minimal pairs from block 2. Mix the 6 sound pairs.
    Examples:
       "I see a ship on the sea."
       "I think the soup is in the sink."
    Bring on paper to session 2.

(3) RECORDING (5-10 min) — record yourself reading
    aloud the 10 connected-speech sentences from
    block 2 dictation. Focus on the contractions
    and the linking. Send to coordination WhatsApp
    before session 2. Label: A1-REEX-1.

RULE: not fragmented. If you do not do the homework,
session 2 opens with a 5-min spoken diagnostic instead
of homework review. Plan B exists — but the work belongs to you.
```

Plan B if the student has no smartphone, no YouTube access, or no WhatsApp: skip task 1 and task 3 — task 2 (writing) is enough alone. Document it in tutor's notebook so coordination knows. Tutor does NOT chase the homework — the protocol is: do it or accept the 5-min spoken diagnostic at start of session 2.

Brief Session 2 announce: *"Session 2 — listening comprehension, question forms, dictation. Same time. Bring your notebook, this homework on paper, your pen. That's it."* No more details — no promise of specific content, no spoilers.

Close: *"Today was day ONE of FORTALEZA. You took the diagnostic. You named what is hard. You stayed in the sound. That is the work. See you session 2."* Two-second pause. *"Coordination handles results. If you ask me about your reexamen grade — ask coordination. My job is the six hours."*

⚠ **ANCHOR (safety):** if block 4 runs short (you're at 8 minutes total because earlier blocks ran long), the priority order is: (1) Phrase close MUST happen — she says it from memory; (2) homework MUST be written on the board and copied — Plan B documented; (3) the three weak points recap can compress to ONE sentence ("the three things from today are X, Y, Z — write them down"); (4) the Session 2 announce can compress to ONE sentence. Never cut homework. Never cut Phrase close.
