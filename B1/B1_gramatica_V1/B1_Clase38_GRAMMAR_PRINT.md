### (3 min) Warm-up — standing modal swap board race
Two columns on the board. Call a past fact + a modal. The first student of each row runs and writes the perfect modal sentence. Cohort verifies. 8 rounds in 3 minutes.

```
"I missed the meeting." + "should"
   → "I should have been at the meeting."
"I missed the meeting." + "could"
   → "I could have called in."
"I drove to the mall." + "shouldn't"
   → "I shouldn't have driven to the mall."
"She didn't reply." + "could"
   → "She could have replied."
"I trusted him." + "shouldn't"
   → "I shouldn't have trusted him."
"He didn't study." + "should"
   → "He should have studied."
"I forgot the file." + "I wish"
   → "I wish I hadn't forgotten the file."
"We watched a bad movie." + "if only"
   → "If only we hadn't watched that movie."
```

Quick note as you erase: *"Same shape every time. MODAL + HAVE + past participle. Never 'should had' — always 'should HAVE.' Never 'I wish I would have' (common North-American slip — accepted but not the textbook form). The textbook form is I WISH I HAD + past participle, same shape as the third conditional IF clause."*

### (2 min) Bridge to today's content
> *"Today's content is M5 FORM: the three perfect modals (could/should/would HAVE + past participle), their negatives (couldn't/shouldn't/wouldn't have), the implied meanings (possible-but-not-done / right-but-not-done / willing-but-not-done), the two regret shortcuts (I WISH I HAD / IF ONLY I HAD), the bridge to the third conditional STRAIGHT shape, and the 3 forbidden moves (no 'should had', no double modal, no 'must' for regret). Then we drill the form physically — 3 zones around the room — and we land it in a 45-minute lessons-learned review of real past decisions. Stay standing."*

## BLOCK 2 — CONTENT OF THE DAY — 40 min

### (8 min) Mini-talk — three perfect modals + regret expressions

Stand at the board. Book p.43, 47 open. Write or have pre-written:

```
M5 — PERFECT MODALS — FORM RULES
(B1 Book p.43 Past Modals · p.47 Regret)

THREE PERFECT MODALS — three meanings:
  COULD HAVE  + past participle = POSSIBLE in the
              past, but NOT done
              "I could have gone to the store."
              (but I didn't)

  SHOULD HAVE + past participle = RECOMMENDED in
              the past, but NOT done (this is
              regret)
              "I should have gone to the store."
              (but I didn't, and I regret it)

  WOULD HAVE  + past participle = WILLING in the
              past, but NOT done
              "I would have gone to the store."
              (but I didn't — usually because
              something stopped me)

ALL THREE — same shape, always:
  SUBJECT + MODAL + HAVE + PAST PARTICIPLE

NEGATIVE forms — same shape + NOT:
  COULDN'T HAVE  = it was NOT possible, so NOT done
                  "I couldn't have helped — I was
                   busy."
  SHOULDN'T HAVE = it was NOT recommended, but
                  WAS done (regret on the other side)
                  "I shouldn't have driven — I was
                   tired."
  WOULDN'T HAVE  = I was NOT willing, so NOT done
                  "I wouldn't have gone — I had work."

REGRET SHORTCUTS (book p.47):
  "I WISH I HAD + past participle"
     "I wish I had gone to the game."
  "IF ONLY I HAD + past participle"
     "If only I had gone to the game."
  Both = "ojalá / si tan solo hubiera ido."
  NEGATIVE regret:
     "I wish I hadn't said that."
     "If only she hadn't left."

BRIDGE TO 3RD CONDITIONAL (from last class):
  STRAIGHT 3rd conditional = perfect modal
  inside a result clause:
     "If I had studied, I would have passed."
                      └─── = "I would have passed"
                            (perfect modal of
                            willingness)

THE 3 FORBIDDEN MOVES:
  ✗ "I should had gone."
     (should HAVE — never should had)
  ✗ "I should must have gone."
     (no double modal — pick one)
  ✗ "I must have gone — but I didn't."
     (must have = certainty deduction from M3,
     NOT regret. Use SHOULD HAVE for regret.)
```

Talk through it standing, mark each rule with your hand. Book p.43 verbatim quote (table headers): *"Could + present perfect → Possible in the past, but not done."* and *"Should + present perfect → Recommendable in the past, but not done."* Book p.47 verbatim: *"'Should/could/would have' are sometimes used to express regrets."* and *"Two other expressions are commonly used to express regret: 1. 'To wish'. Similar to 'ojalá' in Spanish. 2. 'If only'. More common in England."* Book p.44 examples drilled out loud: *"I shouldn't have been angry yesterday. / I should have made tacos for dinner, but I made empanadas. / I should have known you were sick! / Bella should have done her homework last night. / Brian shouldn't have driven to the mall. He should have walked. / You should have seen Natalia last night."* Book p.48 regret examples: *"Regret: I broke the car. → 'I wish I hadn't broken the car.' / Regret: I fought with my mother. → 'If only I hadn't fought with my mother.' / Regret: I forgot my homework. → 'I shouldn't have forgotten my homework!'"*

Phrase of the Day INSERTION #1: *"Could have was possible, should have was right, would have was willing. Fortitude day 3 — name it once, then move."*

### (15 min) Walking transformation — 3 zones

Tape the 3 floor labels in 3 corners (or 3 walls) of the room:
- **COULD HAVE — possible in past, NOT done**
- **SHOULD HAVE — right in past, NOT done (regret)**
- **WOULD HAVE / WISH HAD — willing in past, NOT done**

Stand in the middle. Call a past fact out loud + a meaning tag (possible / right / willing-or-regret). Students physically WALK to the zone that fits + say the full sentence out loud as they arrive. If wrong zone or wrong form, cohort calls "again" and they re-walk. 15 facts in 15 min, ~1 per minute.

```
Past fact called + meaning → Right zone + sentence
1.  "I missed the meeting." (right)
       → SHOULD — "I should have been there."
2.  "I missed the meeting." (willing)
       → WOULD — "I would have come, but I was sick."
3.  "She lost the money." (possible past skill)
       → COULD — "She could have given it to her boss."
4.  "He drove to the mall." (regret)
       → SHOULD — "He shouldn't have driven — he should have walked."
5.  "I forgot the file." (regret WISH)
       → WISH HAD — "I wish I hadn't forgotten the file."
6.  "We watched a bad movie." (regret IF ONLY)
       → WISH HAD — "If only we hadn't watched that movie."
7.  "I didn't sleep enough." (right)
       → SHOULD — "I should have slept more."
8.  "I trusted him." (regret)
       → SHOULD — "I shouldn't have trusted him."
9.  "He didn't reply yesterday." (possible)
       → COULD — "He could have replied."
10. "I ordered the wrong dish." (regret WISH)
       → WISH HAD — "I wish I had ordered the salad."
11. "She didn't say good morning." (possible)
       → COULD — "Anita could have said good morning."
12. "Brian drove to the mall." (regret)
       → SHOULD — "Brian shouldn't have driven."
13. "I went to the beach in cold weather." (regret WISH)
       → WISH HAD — "I wish I hadn't gone to the beach."
14. "I was busy last month." (couldn't = not possible)
       → COULD — "I couldn't have helped — I was too busy."
15. "I didn't tell my mother." (right)
       → SHOULD — "I should have told my mother."
```

Coach: when someone goes to the wrong zone, don't say "wrong" — say "walk it again — was it POSSIBLE, was it RIGHT, or were you WILLING?" When form is right AND zone is right, cohort claps once. Note errors with NAMES in your notebook. Most common slips: "should had" or "I wish I would have." Re-direct to the board.

### (10 min) Pair drilling — fact → regret

Hand each pair: 6 past-fact cards (SET A) + 5 modal+regret cards (SET B). Pair work:

1. Student A picks a past-fact card and reads it: *"I trusted him."*
2. Student B picks ONE modal/regret card and produces a full sentence: *"I shouldn't have trusted him."*
3. Then A reads the SAME fact again and asks B to use a DIFFERENT card — most often I WISH I HAD or IF ONLY I HAD: *"I wish I hadn't trusted him."* (same regret, different shape)
4. Swap roles after every 2 facts. Burn through all 6 facts. Each pair must produce at least 2 sentences with WISH HAD or IF ONLY HAD (regret shortcuts).

Walk and coach the FORM (modal + HAVE never modal + HAD; I WISH I HAD never I WISH I WOULD HAVE in the textbook form). Call out one win publicly per pair. Phrase of the Day INSERTION #2: *"Could have, should have, would have — name it once. Fortitude day 3 — and we don't stay stuck."*

### (7 min) Podium — reinforce I WISH / IF ONLY

Draw a podium on the board (1st, 2nd, 3rd). Pick 3 strong students to come up one at a time. Each gets a regret prompt; produces the full sentence; cohort confirms in chorus *"Confirmed!"* If they hesitate or get it wrong, the cohort gives the chorus correction.

```
1st (I WISH I HAD — positive regret):
   "You didn't accept a job offer last year."
       → "I wish I had accepted the offer."

2nd (IF ONLY I HADN'T — negative regret):
   "You answered an angry email at 11 PM."
       → "If only I hadn't answered that email."

3rd (SHOULDN'T HAVE — direct):
   "Your colleague drove a friend home drunk."
       → "He shouldn't have driven."
```

Then one final chorus drill — call a past fact, cohort responds in unison with the regret form you specify:

> *"I forgot the file."* (SHOULD) → "I should have remembered the file."
> *"I forgot the file."* (WISH) → "I wish I hadn't forgotten the file."
> *"I drove tired."* (SHOULDN'T) → "I shouldn't have driven tired."

Note: *"Stand up — we move to the simulation."*
