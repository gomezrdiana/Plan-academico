### (3 min) Warm-up — standing certainty echo
Two columns on the board. Call a clue out loud. The first student of each row runs and writes ONE deduction at the level you call (very sure / half sure / very sure opposite). Cohort verifies. 8 rounds in 3 minutes.

```
"His car is here." (very sure)
   → "He must be here."
"His car is here." (half sure)
   → "He might be here." / "He could be here."
"His car is here." (very sure opposite)
   → "He can't be away."
"The lights are off." (half)
   → "Nobody may be home."
"She is wearing a coat." (very sure)
   → "It must be cold."
"There is no reply." (half)
   → "He might be busy."
"The mug is warm." (very sure past)
   → "Someone must have made coffee."
"His mic is muted." (very sure opposite)
   → "He can't have heard the question."
```

Quick note as you erase: *"Notice — when the deduction is about now we use must / might / can't + BASE verb. When it's about the past we add HAVE + past participle. Same certainty levels, different time."*

### (2 min) Bridge to today's content
> *"Today's content is M3 FORM: the three certainty levels (very sure / partial / very sure opposite), the present form (modal + base verb), the past form (modal + have + past participle), the 4 forbidden moves (no 'maybe' instead of the modal, no 'don't can', no double modal, no 'must' for opposite — use can't), and the listening trick to spot which level a colleague is using. Then we drill the form physically — 5 zones on a certainty line around the room — and we land it in a 45-minute inference briefing where you infer what happened from a clue pack. Stay standing."*

## BLOCK 2 — CONTENT OF THE DAY — 40 min

### (8 min) Mini-talk — three certainty levels + present and past

Stand at the board. Book p.23 open. Write or have pre-written:

```
M3 — DEDUCTION — FORM RULES (B1 Book p.23)

THREE LEVELS OF CERTAINTY — three verbs:
  100% YES   → MUST                 ("He must be sick.")
  ~50% YES   → MIGHT / MAY / COULD  ("He might be sick.")
  100% NO    → CAN'T / MUSTN'T      ("He can't be sick.")

PRESENT — modal + BASE verb:
  "He must be at the office."
  "She might know the answer."
  "It can't be true."

PAST — modal + HAVE + PAST PARTICIPLE:
  "He must have arrived already."
  "She might have called."
  "It can't have been him."

CONTINUOUS (book note — common with TO BE):
  "He must be playing soccer."  (now)
  "He must be sleeping."        (now)
  "He must have been working."  (past)

THE 4 FORBIDDEN MOVES:
  ✗ "Maybe he is sick."         (use the modal)
  ✗ "He don't can be at home."  (no double aux)
  ✗ "He must can be sick."      (no double modal)
  ✗ "He must not be sick."      (for "100% NO" use
                                 CAN'T, not mustn't —
                                 mustn't = prohibition)

LISTENING TRICK — what level is your colleague?
  hears "must" → they sound CONFIDENT, agree or
                 push back with evidence
  hears "might / may / could" → they are GUESSING,
                 you can offer evidence to confirm
  hears "can't" → they're RULING IT OUT, you can
                 give counter-evidence if you have it
```

Talk through it standing, mark each rule with your hand. Book p.23 verbatim quote: *"We use verbs of deduction to express a supposition (something we think or believe). The verb depends on the certainty the speaker feels."* Book p.23 verbatim note: *"It is very common to use deduction with the verb 'to be'. We often use it in the continuous and perfect tenses."* Book p.24 examples drilled out loud: *"Horst is late. He must be sick. / Hamlet is sad. His father must have died. / It is raining today. We may arrive late. / Mike is here. He can't be sick. / Sam must have arrived last night. His car is here. / Theo knows the answer. He may have asked the teacher. / I hear music. The concert must have started. / The boys can't be playing soccer. I have their ball."*

Phrase of the Day INSERTION #1: *"Must is certainty, might could may is partial, can't is the opposite. Fortitude day 1 — we keep talking even when we are not sure."*

### (15 min) Walking transformation — 5 zones on a certainty line

Tape the 5 floor labels in a STRAIGHT LINE on one wall (or across the floor):
- **100% YES — MUST**
- **60% YES — MIGHT / MAY / COULD**
- **0% — CAN'T / MUSTN'T**
- **100% YES — PAST — MUST HAVE**
- **0% — PAST — CAN'T HAVE**

Stand in the middle. Call a clue out loud + a time (now / past) + a level (sure / partial / sure-opposite). Students physically WALK to the zone that fits + say the full deduction sentence out loud as they arrive. If wrong zone or wrong tense, cohort calls "again" and they re-walk. 16 clues in 15 min, ~1 per minute.

```
Clue called → time + level → Right zone + right form
1.  "His car is here." now / sure
       → MUST — "He must be here."
2.  "His car is here." now / partial
       → MIGHT — "He might be here."
3.  "His car is in the garage." now / sure-opp
       → CAN'T — "He can't have left."
4.  "Tania finished her HW." past / sure
       → MUST HAVE — "Her mother must have helped her."
5.  "I hear music." past / sure
       → MUST HAVE — "The concert must have started."
6.  "His mic is muted." past / sure-opp
       → CAN'T HAVE — "He can't have heard the question."
7.  "There is no reply." now / partial
       → MIGHT — "He might be in a meeting."
8.  "The door is open." past / sure
       → MUST HAVE — "Someone must have entered."
9.  "She is smiling." now / sure
       → MUST — "She must be happy."
10. "He is sleeping at noon." now / partial
       → MAY — "He may be sick."
11. "His chair is empty at 9 AM." now / partial
       → COULD — "He could be running late."
12. "My son's phone is dead." past / sure-opp
       → CAN'T HAVE — "He can't have called me."
13. "Lights off." now / sure-opp
       → CAN'T — "There can't be anyone inside."
14. "Receipt is signed." past / sure
       → MUST HAVE — "He must have approved it."

       → MUST HAVE — "It must have rained."
```

Coach: when someone goes to the wrong zone, don't say "wrong" — say "walk it again — what's the certainty level?" When form is right AND zone is right, cohort claps once. Note errors with NAMES in your notebook. Most common slip: "He don't can be here" or "Maybe he is here." Re-direct to the modal.

### (10 min) Pair drilling — clue → deduction

Hand each pair: 6 clue cards (SET A) + 5 deduction verb cards (SET B). Pair work:

1. Student A picks a clue card and reads it: *"The mug is warm."*
2. Student B picks ONE deduction verb card and produces a full sentence at that level: *"Someone must have made coffee."*
3. Then A picks the SAME clue and challenges B to do a PARTIAL version: *"Someone might have just left."* (different modal, same clue)
4. Swap roles after every 2 clues. Burn through all 6 clues. Each pair must produce at least 2 PAST deductions ("must have / can't have / might have").

Walk and coach the FORM (modal + base for now, modal + have + past participle for past, never "maybe" instead of the modal). Call out one win publicly per pair. Phrase of the Day INSERTION #2: *"Must is certainty, might could may is partial, can't is the opposite. Fortitude day 1 — we keep talking."*

### (7 min) Podium — reinforce the perfect modal pull-out

Draw a podium on the board (1st, 2nd, 3rd). Pick 3 strong students to come up one at a time. Each gets a PAST clue; produces the full past-deduction sentence; cohort confirms in chorus *"Confirmed!"* If they hesitate or get it wrong, the cohort gives the chorus correction.

```
1st (MUST HAVE):
   "Sam's car is in the driveway and the engine
    is warm."
       → "Sam must have arrived a few minutes ago."

2nd (MIGHT HAVE):
   "There's an empty plate on the table."
       → "Someone might have eaten lunch here."

3rd (CAN'T HAVE):
   "My son didn't have his phone yesterday."
       → "He can't have called me yesterday."
```

Then one final chorus drill — call the present version of each clue, cohort responds with the PAST version in unison:

> *"Sam is at home."* (past) → "Sam must have arrived."
> *"He's eating."* (past) → "He must have eaten."
> *"She's calling."* (past, ruled out) → "She can't have called."

Note: *"Stand up — we move to the simulation."*

## BLOCK 3 — LIVE APPLICATION — 45 min — "INFERENCE BRIEFING ROOM"

ONE activity, no format change for 45 minutes. Rotating groups of 3-4. Every student plays every role.

### (3 min) Setup
> *"You're running an INFERENCE BRIEFING ROOM. Groups of 3-4, rotating roles. Each group gets a CLUE PACK — 6 facts from a missing scenario (a project log gap, an email thread with two emails missing, a meeting that ended early). The BRIEFER reads the clues one at a time. The ANALYSTS take turns producing full deduction sentences — present OR past — at one of the three certainty levels. The OBSERVER marks each deduction on the grid — right modal for the level, right tense for the time, correct form (no 'maybe', no 'don't can'). Three rounds with three different clue packs. Each round 12 minutes. Every student plays every role exactly once. I will WRITE recurring errors on the side of the board live — when there are three on the board we stop and correct as a cohort."*

Hand out the 3 printed clue packs:

```
4. The kitchen coffee machine was used at 5:30 PM.
5. Someone signed for a package at the reception
   at 5:45 PM.
```

### (~40 min) 3 rounds × ~12 min — rotating groups + live board errors

**Round 1 (12 min):** Groups form. Each group gets clue pack A. Briefer reads clues; analysts respond in 3 sec with full deductions; observer marks. You circulate and WRITE EVERY RECURRING ERROR on the side of the board as you hear it. When 3 errors are on the board, freeze — cohort corrects in chorus 2×, erase, continue.

**Round 2 (12 min):** Rotate roles. Groups switch to clue pack B. Push for at least 3 PAST deductions per group ("must have / might have / can't have"). Continue live board-error correction. Phrase of the Day INSERTION #3: *"Must is certainty, might could may is partial, can't is the opposite — past or present. Fortitude day 1."*

**Round 3 (12 min):** Final rotation. Groups switch to clue pack C. Push for at least 2 CAN'T / CAN'T HAVE per group — they avoid the negative deduction. By now they should be sharp. Slow down only for the most stubborn pattern you wrote on the board across the previous 2 rounds.

Write on the board (anonymous; NAMES only in your personal notebook):

```
ERROR PAPER — CL 36 GRAMMAR

1. ❌ "Maybe he is sick."
   → ✅ "He MIGHT be sick." / "He MAY be sick."
     (use the modal — "maybe" is an adverb, not the
     deduction structure)

2. ❌ "He must not be at home."     (for "certainly not")
   → ✅ "He CAN'T be at home."
     (must not / mustn't = prohibition. For 100%
     NO certainty use CAN'T.)

3. ❌ "He must arrived already."
   → ✅ "He must HAVE arrived already."
     (past deduction = modal + HAVE + past part.)

4. ❌ "He don't can be sick."
   → ✅ "He CAN'T be sick."
     (modals never take do/don't — they are their
     own auxiliary)
```
