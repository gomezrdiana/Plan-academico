## 2. CONTENT — PRESENT SIMPLE FOR REAL ROUTINES — 40 min — PAIRS

### Mini-talk — 6 min — STAND, then sit with partner

Write on the board (before class):

```
PRESENT SIMPLE — REAL CONVERSATION

Shape:    I / you / we / they + VERB
          he / she / it       + VERB-S

Examples: "I always check email at 8."
          "She usually arrives by 9."
          "They never close on Sundays."

Frequency adverbs go:
   BEFORE the main verb:   "I ALWAYS confirm by email."
   AFTER the verb BE:      "She IS USUALLY on time."

Common adverbs (most → least often):
   always · usually · often · sometimes · rarely · never
```

Say at the board:
> *"Present Simple is not the verb of 'right now.' It's the verb of YOUR PATTERN. 'I check email at 8' — that's every day, not this morning. Frequency adverbs answer HOW OFTEN. Position rule: BEFORE the main verb ('I ALWAYS confirm'), but AFTER the verb BE ('she IS USUALLY on time'). Third person — he / she / it — adds -s. We will hear that gap a lot today and we will name it cleanly. The Grammar Teacher formalizes the full table this morning — we name the shape orally now, he writes the table later."*

Model 3 sentences yourself, real ones from your week:
- *"I usually check Slack before email."*
- *"My coworker rarely takes the 7 AM bus."*
- *"Our team never schedules meetings on Friday afternoon."*

### Guided practice — partner workweek interview — 18 min — pairs sit

> *"Find a partner. One you have not worked with today. Sit knee-to-knee. ENGLISH ONLY for the next 18 minutes."*

Write on the board:

```
PARTNER WORKWEEK INTERVIEW — 18 min total
                              (9 min each direction)

Ask your partner about their typical work week.
Use Present Simple. Use 5 different frequency
adverbs at least once each across all questions.

Sample question shapes (use any — invent more):
   "What time do you usually start work?"
   "How often do you check email in the morning?"
   "Who do you always talk to before lunch?"
   "What do you sometimes forget to do before
    leaving the office?"
   "When do you rarely answer the phone?"

REQUIREMENTS:
   - 6+ questions per direction
   - Partner answers in FULL sentences with
     Present Simple + frequency adverb
   - Listen for: third-person -s missing /
     adverb in wrong position / question with
     "DOES" + verb-S (both is wrong — only ONE
     gets the -s)
```

Round 1 — 9 min. Partner A asks; Partner B answers full sentences. Conversational Teacher circulates, listens, coaches one-on-one. Correction technique: *"Listen — 'she USUALLY arrives' — say it."* Cohort says it twice. Note errors in Error Paper anonymously, NAMES in personal notebook.

Round 2 — 9 min. Switch. Partner B asks; Partner A answers.

**Insertion #1 of Phrase of the Day:** during round 2, walk by the most-confident pair and drop quietly: *"Steady pattern, not dramatic exception — that's exactly what you're naming."*

### Free pair derivation — true/false routines — 13 min — pairs sit

> *"Stay with your partner. New task: each of you writes mentally 3 sentences about YOUR routine — 2 TRUE, 1 FALSE. Use 3 different frequency adverbs. 'I always have coffee before 7.' 'I usually walk to work.' 'I never check my phone before bed.' Partner guesses which one is the FALSE. Then partner asks ONE follow-up question. 'Why never?' 'How often is usually?' Then switch."*

Setup — 2 min: model with one student. Show TRUE / TRUE / FALSE rhythm. Show the follow-up question shape (DO / DOES + subject + base verb).

Run — 9 min: 3 full rotations between partners.

Whole-cohort share — 2 min: pull 3 random students; each gives ONE strong sentence from their partner ("She told me she NEVER eats breakfast — that surprised me"). Applause.

**Insertion #2 of Phrase of the Day:** *"Right subject, right verb shape, right frequency adverb — that's the temperate professional naming their week."*

### Whole-group share — 3 min — sit then stand to share

Pull 3 students at random; each gives 2 strong sentences they SAID (not their partner). Listen for 3rd-person -s, adverb position, question form. Note gaps for Block 3 setup. Stand when sharing.

## 3. LIVE APPLICATION — "CAREER FAIR WORKWEEK INTERVIEW" — 45 min — STAND UP

### Setup — 3 min

> *"Stand up. Push chairs to the walls. A career fair scene — a recruiter is screening candidates with workweek questions. Guest Observer pattern (validated): one INTERVIEWER, one INTERVIEWEE (the candidate), one OBSERVER who takes notes. Rotate every ~12 min so each of you plays all three roles. I coach from the side — I never play interviewer."*

Board:

```
"THE CAREER FAIR WORKWEEK INTERVIEW"
3 rounds · ~12 min each · TRIADS of 3

PATTERN VALIDATED (Guest Observer):
  - 1 INTERVIEWER (rotates each round — plays
    recruiter)
  - 1 INTERVIEWEE (the candidate)
  - 1 OBSERVER (counts correct Present Simple +
    frequency adverbs + 3rd-person -s; flags
    missed -s, wrong adverb position, double-
    inflection "does she works")
  - PROFESSOR (coach, never plays interviewer)

INTERVIEWER SCRIPT (5 questions + 1 follow-up
                    per round):
  Q1 — "Tell me about your typical morning at
        work. What time do you usually start?"
  Q2 — "How often do you take work home in the
        evening?"
  Q3 — "Who do you always coordinate with
        before a big deadline?"
  Q4 — "What do you rarely have time for during
        the work week?"
  Q5 — "When do you usually feel most focused?"
  Follow-up — any question starting with WHY
              or HOW that pushes for one more
              detail in Present Simple.

INTERVIEWEE TARGET (answering):
  - Full sentences in Present Simple
  - 5 different frequency adverbs across the
    interview (always / usually / often /
    sometimes / never)
  - Third-person -s when describing a coworker
    or team

OBSERVER NOTES (silent):
  - Strong: 2 sentences that were specific,
    correct shape, clear adverb position
  - One specific gap: "missed -s on 'she
    arrive'" / "adverb in wrong place — 'she
    arrives usually' should be 'she usually
    arrives'" / "double inflection — 'does she
    works' should be 'does she work'"
```

### Model — 3 min — at the front

> *"Let me model the interviewer. 'Tell me about your typical morning. What time do you usually start?' Listen for the interviewee answering 'I usually start at 8.' If they say 'I usually starting' — that's a B1 gap, we name it after. If they say 'she USUALLY arrive' — missed -s. Observer would write that down. I do NOT play interviewer in the actual rounds — you do."*

Pick 2 volunteers, do one 90-second demo at the front.

### Round 1 — 12 min

Form triads (5 students would mean 1 triad of 3 + 1 pair → use a pair: interviewer + interviewee, no observer; cohort acts as observer for the pair). Each interviewer asks the 5 questions + follow-up. Interviewee answers full sentences. Observer takes notes. Conversational Teacher circulates and coaches softly — corrects shape, not explains grammar. Take literal errors into the Error Paper (anonymous) and personal notebook (with NAMES).

### Round 2 — 12 min

ROTATE roles. Each student now plays a different role. Same script. Different specifics from their own week.

### Round 3 — 12 min

ROTATE again so each student plays the third role. Same script.

### Cohort close — 3 min

Each triad delivers ONE strong sentence from their interviewee — said aloud. Applause. Note the strongest 3-4 sentences on the board.

**Insertion #3 of Phrase of the Day:** *"You just spoke that — steady pattern across three roles, not dramatic exception. That is temperance day 2 in the mouth."*

## 4. CLOSE — Error Paper + Phrase close + Tarea + PASE to Grammar — 15 min — STAND UP

### Error Paper Live — 4 min

Write on the board (anonymous; NAMES only in personal notebook):

```
ERRORS HEARD TODAY:
1. ❌ "She arrive at 9 every Monday."        → ✅ "She ARRIVES at 9 every Monday."         (third-person -s)
2. ❌ "I go usually to the office at 8."     → ✅ "I USUALLY go to the office at 8."       (adverb BEFORE main verb)
3. ❌ "Does she works on Fridays?"           → ✅ "Does she WORK on Fridays?"              (DOES already inflects — verb stays BASE)
4. ❌ "He don't check email after 6."        → ✅ "He DOESN'T check email after 6."        (third-person negative = DOESN'T)
```
