## BLOCK 2 — CONTENT OF THE DAY — 40 min

### (5 min) Mini-talk — three certainty levels in workplace dialogue

Talk standing, board behind you. Model EACH level out loud.

> *"Deduction in real work dialogue — three certainty levels, one verb pattern. Level ONE: VERY CERTAIN — 'must'. 'Maria isn't on the call — she must be sick.' 'Must' + BASE form, no 'to', no -ing. 'He must work late' — not 'must to work', not 'must working'. Level TWO: PARTIALLY CERTAIN — 'might', 'may', 'could'. Same pattern — 'might be', 'may have', 'could know'. These three are interchangeable in deduction; British English prefers 'may', North American prefers 'might' or 'could'. Level THREE: VERY CERTAIN OF THE OPPOSITE — 'can't' or 'mustn't'. NEVER 'don't must'. The negative for certainty is 'can't' — 'he can't be sick, he just ran 14 km'. Critical preview: when the deduction is about the PAST, we add 'have' + participle. 'He must HAVE been sick yesterday.' 'They might HAVE gone home.' 'She can't HAVE called — her phone was dead.' Fortitude day 1: the work is to make the call with incomplete information. 'I don't know' is the easy answer. The level of certainty named out loud is the hard one — and it's the one we want."*

Demo on the board:

```
PRESENT DEDUCTION (right now)
  MUST + base      → 90% sure          "He must be tired."
  MIGHT/MAY/COULD  → 50% guess         "He might be tired."
  CAN'T / MUSTN'T  → 90% sure opposite "He can't be tired."

PAST DEDUCTION (preview — full in Cl 38)
  MUST  HAVE + participle    "He must have left."
  MIGHT HAVE + participle    "He might have left."
  CAN'T HAVE + participle    "He can't have left."

NEVER: "must to be" · "can't to be" · "don't must"
```

### (15 min) Guided practice in pairs — 12 mystery-clue cards

Hand each pair the deck of 12 mystery-clue cards. They take turns: one reads the clue aloud, the other gives THREE deductions — one for each certainty level (must / might or could / can't). Then swap. Walk between pairs and physically correct on the spot — especially "must to be", missing "have" on past deductions, and "don't must" for the negative.

Card deck (one clue per card):
```
1.  "Andres usually opens the office at 7 — today it was locked at 9."
2.  "The board meeting was supposed to last 1 hour — it's now 3 hours in."
3.  "The Q3 report has the wrong currency on every page."
4.  "The new intern is asking everyone where the bathroom is."
5.  "Maria's laptop is on, her coffee is hot, her chair is empty."
6.  "The client signed the proposal — but didn't send the PO."
7.  "The team standup got cancelled twice this week with no reason."
8.  "The CEO's calendar shows a flight to Bogotá on Tuesday."
9.  "The dev branch has 200 commits from one person overnight."
10. "The receptionist is wearing a suit — she usually wears jeans."
11. "Every desk in the marketing area is empty at 2 PM."
12. "The same Uber Eats order has been placed every day this week."
```

Coach softly while you walk: missing "have" on past deductions, "must to / can't to" with infinitive, "don't must" for negative, mixing up "may" (deduction) with "may" (polite ask). Note errors with NAMES in your notebook. Phrase of the Day INSERTION #2: *"Fortitude day 1 — name the certainty, don't hide in 'I don't know'."*

## BLOCK 3 — LIVE APPLICATION — 45 min — "OFFICE WHO-DID-IT / INFERENCE CALL"

ONE activity, no format change for 45 minutes. Rotating triads. Every student plays every role.

### (3 min) Setup
> *"Stand. You're three colleagues on a Friday afternoon, trying to figure out three things that went wrong at the office this week — a project that slipped, a meeting that ran late, and an email that got lost. You'll work in triads — three roles, rotating. INVESTIGATOR opens with the mystery and runs the conversation. COLLEAGUE A gives 4 deductions — mixing present and past, mixing high and low certainty. COLLEAGUE B gives 4 deductions on the SAME mysteries — must include 1 'can't' or 'mustn't' and 1 past 'must have' or 'might have'. So the round is 8 deductions total. OBSERVER tracks 3 things on the grid I'm handing out: how many deductions use the correct verb pattern (base form, no 'to'), how many past deductions include 'have' + participle, and whether each speaker NAMED THE LEVEL of certainty or hid in 'I don't know'. Three rounds. Each round 12 minutes. Every student plays every role exactly once. Fortitude day 1: name the call, even if it's wrong."*

### (~40 min) 3 rounds × ~12 min — rotating triads

**Round 1 (12 min):** Triads form. Roles assigned. INVESTIGATOR sets the mystery — "the Q3 report has the wrong currency on every page, what happened?". A and B alternate, 8 deductions total. Observer marks the grid. You circulate triad by triad — coach the verb pattern when wrong, kill "must to be" instantly, push for past deductions when the group sticks to present.

**Round 2 (12 min):** Rotate. New INVESTIGATOR, new A, new B, new OBSERVER. Same 8-deduction structure on a NEW mystery (the meeting that ran 2 hours over). By now the cohort knows the rhythm — push for SPEED. Deductions should land in 4 seconds. Push for at least 3 past deductions per round.

**Round 3 (12 min):** Final rotation. Every student has now played every role. Push for COMPLEXITY: at least one CAN'T or MUSTN'T per deduction speaker, and at least one full sequence "might have done X, must have done Y, can't have done Z" from the same person about the same mystery.

Phrase of the Day INSERTION #4 during round 2 transition: *"Fortitude day 1 — when the facts run out, the level of certainty is the work. Name it."*

### (2 min) Cohort close
