## BLOCK 2 — CONTENT OF THE DAY — 40 min

### (5 min) Mini-talk — five perfect modals + two regret expressions

Talk standing, board behind you. Model EACH meaning out loud.

> *"Five perfect modals. Same form. Different meaning. Form first — every single one is modal + HAVE + past participle. 'should have been', 'could have done', 'would have called', 'might have known', 'must have left'. The 'have' is not optional. Meanings — and this is where Spanish speakers fall. SHOULD HAVE: recommendation in retrospect — critique. 'You should have called.' (You didn't call. I'm telling you that was wrong.) COULD HAVE: past possibility not taken. 'You could have called.' (It was possible. I'm not blaming, just naming the option.) WOULD HAVE: willingness, not exercised. 'I would have called, but my phone died.' (The intention was there, the action wasn't.) MIGHT HAVE: lower possibility. 'You might have called.' (Maybe — partial certainty about a possible past action.) MUST HAVE: deduction about the past — we previewed this in Cl 36. 'You must have called — there's a missed call from you.' (90% sure based on evidence.) Plus the regret expressions. 'I WISH I HAD' = ojalá hubiera. 'IF ONLY I HAD' = si tan sólo hubiera — more dramatic, more common in British English. Same form as third conditional IF clause — HAD + participle. WATCH: North American speakers say 'I wish I would have' — book says it's incorrect but generally accepted. We teach the correct form — 'I wish I had'. Fortitude day 3: name the regret, walk forward. The form is the same. The meaning is what we're choosing."*

Demo on the board:

```
PERFECT MODALS — same form, different meaning
  WOULD HAVE  + participle → willingness not exercised
  COULD HAVE  + participle → past possibility not taken
  SHOULD HAVE + participle → critique / recommendation
  MIGHT HAVE  + participle → lower past possibility
  MUST HAVE   + participle → past deduction (from Cl 36)

REGRET EXPRESSIONS — same "HAD + participle" as 3rd cond
  "I wish I HAD called you."        = ojalá hubiera llamado
  "If only I HAD called you."       = si tan sólo hubiera llamado

NEVER: "I should called" · "I wish I would have called" (book p.47: accepted but technically wrong)
```

Hand each pair the deck of 12 regret/critique cards. They take turns: one reads the situation aloud, the other gives THREE responses — one with a perfect modal of CRITIQUE (should/shouldn't have), one with a perfect modal of POSSIBILITY (could have / might have), one with a REGRET expression (I wish I had / If only I had). Then swap. Walk between pairs and physically correct on the spot.

```
3.  "You didn't proofread the email before sending."

6.  "You ate sushi from the gas station."
7.  "Maria didn't push back on the unrealistic deadline."
8.  "The dev branch wasn't backed up."
9.  "You bought the cheap laptop."
10. "The intern fixed the bug on production at 2 AM."
11. "You took the contract without legal review."
12. "The team skipped the retro."
```

Coach softly while you walk: "I should called" (missing have), "I wish I would have" (incorrect form), confusion between SHOULD HAVE (critique) and COULD HAVE (possibility), missing the regret meaning ("I wish I had a coffee" — that's present hypothetical, not past regret). Note errors with NAMES in your notebook. Phrase of the Day INSERTION #2: *"Fortitude day 3 — regret is data. Name it cleanly. Should have, could have, wish I had."*

## BLOCK 3 — LIVE APPLICATION — 45 min — "LESSONS-LEARNED REVIEW"

ONE activity, no format change for 45 minutes. Rotating pairs (then triads in round 3). Every student speaks every round.

### (3 min) Setup
> *"Stand. You're in a quarterly lessons-learned review — two colleagues sitting down to look at the last 90 days of decisions, name what they wish they had done differently, and extract the rules for next quarter. You'll work in pairs for rounds 1 and 2, then triads for round 3. SPEAKER picks ONE real past work decision and gives 6 perfect modals + 2 regret expressions on it. LISTENER asks 1 follow-up per sentence ('why did that matter?', 'what would have changed?', 'what's the rule now?'). At the end of the 12 minutes, SPEAKER must name the 'Today I…' carry-forward — the rule extracted. Then swap. Round 3 adds a third role — WITNESS — who observes silently with the grid. Three rounds. Each round 12 minutes. Every student speaks at least twice. Fortitude day 3 — regret is data, walk forward."*

### (~40 min) 3 rounds × ~12 min — rotating pairs/triads

**Round 1 (12 min):** Pairs form. Roles assigned. SPEAKER picks a past decision, runs 6 perfect modals + 2 regret expressions, LISTENER asks follow-ups. No grid yet — both partners just speak. You circulate pair by pair — coach the form when wrong, kill "I wish I would have" instantly (*"redo: 'I wish I HAD'"*), push for the "Today I…" carry-forward at the 10-min mark.

**Round 2 (12 min):** Swap. Other partner now speaks. Same 6+2 structure. By now the cohort knows the rhythm — push for VARIETY of modals (most cohorts default to SHOULD HAVE only — push for COULD HAVE, MIGHT HAVE, MUST HAVE). Push for at least 1 "If only I had" (most students stick to "I wish I had" — the more dramatic regret is harder).

**Round 3 (12 min):** Form triads. New pair + a WITNESS. WITNESS marks the observer grid silently. SPEAKER runs the full 8 (6 modals + 2 regrets) + the carry-forward at the end. After 8 minutes, WITNESS reads back from the grid — what landed, what didn't, what was the strongest carry-forward.

Phrase of the Day INSERTION #4 during round 2 transition: *"Fortitude day 3 — the regret is the data, the carry-forward is the work. Both, out loud."*

### (2 min) Cohort close
One strong triad demos 90 sec in front of the group — SPEAKER runs 2 modals + 1 regret + the carry-forward, WITNESS reports from the grid. Applause.

> *"Fortitude day three. Two more days in the block. Today you named past decisions and turned them into rules. That's the work."*
