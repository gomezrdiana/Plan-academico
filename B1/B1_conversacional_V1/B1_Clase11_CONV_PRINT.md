### 5b. Intro Present Perfect formal (2.5 min) — SENTADAS

**OBJETIVO PARA LA ESTUDIANTE:**
> *"Today we open a new tense — Present Perfect. The tense that distinguishes B1 from A2. We use it for life experiences and for actions that started in the past but still matter NOW."*

**Lee EN INGLÉS:**

> *"Present Perfect = HAVE / HAS + past participle.*
>
> *Regular verbs: -ed (worked, studied, traveled).*
> *Irregular verbs: a list to learn (seen, gone, done, been, known, taken, given, written).*
>
> *Three uses you'll see today in the text:*
>
> *1. Life experience: 'I HAVE traveled to Cartagena.' (no specific time)*
> *2. Continuing action: 'She HAS lived here since 2018.' (started past, still now)*
> *3. Recent action with present relevance: 'He HAS just finished.' (recent + relevant)*
>
> *Contrast with Past Simple: 'I WENT to Cartagena in 2019.' (specific time = Past Simple).*
>
> *Today's text uses Present Perfect 12+ times. Find them as you read."*

## First-Time Experiences That Have Shaped Us

Every adult I **have** ever interviewed remembers at least one first-time experience that **has** changed them irreversibly. Some are obvious — the first day at university, the first job, the first time leaving the country. Others are more **counterintuitive** — a **peculiar** conversation with a stranger that **has** stayed with them for decades, or an **uncomfortable** silence at a dinner that taught them more than any lecture.

I **have** collected stories from over a hundred people, and the **empirical** pattern is **irrefutable**: the experiences that **have** shaped us most are rarely the ones we planned. They are **asymmetric** — small in the moment, **indispensable** in retrospect.

Maria **has** told me she still remembers the first time she gave a presentation in English to a foreign client. She **was** 24. She **had** never spoken to a foreigner alone before, and she **felt** utterly **vulnerable**. The presentation itself **was mediocre** — she **has** admitted that herself. But the experience **compelled** her to enroll in an English program, and now, eight years later, she negotiates international contracts every week.

Carlos, who I **have** known since university, **shared** something different. He **had** always been confident, but a single feedback session with a senior mentor in 2018 **changed** his trajectory. The mentor **said**, "Your work is good, but it **incentivizes** the wrong behavior in your team." It **was compelling** — and **uncomfortable**. Carlos **has** rebuilt his leadership style since then. He says that conversation **has** been more useful than any course he **has** taken.

The lesson is simple, even if it sounds **counterintuitive**: you don't need to plan transformative experiences. You need to be open to them when they arrive. Some will feel **peculiar** at first, others **uncomfortable**. Many will leave you **vulnerable**. But these are precisely the moments that, looking back, you'll realize **have** shaped who you **have** become.

## GRAMMAR FOCUS IN THIS TEXT

**M16 — Present Perfect (foco principal de hoy)**

Forms in the text (regular + irregular):
- *"have ever interviewed"* (regular: interviewed)
- *"has changed them"* (regular)
- *"has stayed with them"* (regular)
- *"I have collected stories"* (regular)
- *"have shaped us"* (regular)
- *"Maria has told me"* (irregular: told)
- *"She has admitted"* (regular)
- *"I have known since"* (irregular: known)
- *"Carlos has rebuilt"* (irregular: rebuilt)
- *"has been more useful"* (irregular: been)
- *"any course he has taken"* (irregular: taken)
- *"have shaped who you have become"* (regular + irregular: become)

**M17 — Present Perfect vs Past Simple (contraste explícito)**

Examples of the contrast in the text:
- *"She HAS admitted that herself"* (PP — relevance now) vs *"The presentation itself WAS mediocre"* (PS — past complete)
- *"Carlos HAS rebuilt his leadership style"* (PP — ongoing relevance) vs *"a single feedback session... CHANGED his trajectory"* (PS — specific past moment)
- *"I HAVE known Carlos since university"* (PP — continuing) vs *"he SHARED something different"* (PS — past event)

**Past Perfect (bonus exposure, no need to teach today):**
- *"She had never spoken to a foreigner alone before"*
- *"He had always been confident"*

## SUGGESTED USES IN CLASS

1. **Silent read + paired re-read** — 5 min. Estudiantes leen primero solos, luego en parejas en voz alta.
2. **Find and underline** — encontrar TODOS los Present Perfect (hay 12+) y TODOS los Past Simple (hay 8+).
3. **Categorize the verbs** — separar Present Perfect en (a) regulares y (b) irregulares. Construir lista en tablero.
4. **Vocabulary recall check** — leer las 11 palabras en este nuevo contexto. Preguntar: *"What did this word mean when we read it on Tuesday in 'Mentors'? Same meaning here?"* — fuerza consolidación de Tuesday's text.
5. **Contrast drill (M17)** — escoger 3 oraciones donde PP y PS coexisten. Preguntar: *"Why Present Perfect here? Why Past Simple there?"*
6. **Oral summary** — cada estudiante resume el texto en 3 frases usando Present Perfect.
7. **Personal application** — cada estudiante construye 1 párrafo similar sobre UN first-time experience suyo.
8. **Debate (Hot Topic)** — *"Is the author right that unplanned experiences shape us more than planned ones?"*
