### 00:00--00:20 | ICEBREAKER: NEVER HAVE I EVER (20 min)

Say: "Welcome back. Today we're going deeper into perfect tenses. But first --- a game. It's called Never Have I Ever. Here's how it works. Everyone holds up 5 fingers. I say a sentence starting with 'Never have I ever...' If you HAVE done that thing, you put one finger down. The last person with fingers up wins."

**Demo first.** Say: "I'll start. Never have I ever... eaten sushi for breakfast." Look around. Say: "If you HAVE eaten sushi for breakfast --- put a finger down. If you haven't --- keep it up."

Say: "See? The sentence uses the present perfect. 'Never have I ever eaten.' 'Have eaten.' That's what we're practicing. Your turn. Go around the circle. Each person says one 'Never have I ever' sentence."

**Round 1 (8 min):** Go around the room. Each student says one sentence. If someone says "Never have I ever go to Japan," say: "No. Listen. Never have I ever GONE to Japan." They repeat. Move on.

Common errors to listen for:
- "Never I have" instead of "Never have I" --- say: "No. NEVER HAVE I EVER. The order changes after 'never.' Say it again."
- "Never have I ever went" instead of "gone" --- say: "No. Gone. Never have I ever GONE."
- "Never have I ever eat" instead of "eaten" --- say: "No. Eaten. Say it again."

**Round 2 (7 min):** Say: "New rule. After someone puts a finger down, they have to tell the story. But the story must use PAST SIMPLE. Watch --- if I put my finger down for 'Never have I ever broken a bone,' I say: 'I broke my arm when I was 12. I fell off my bicycle.' See? Present perfect for the experience, past simple for the details."

Go around again. When a student puts a finger down, they tell the short story (3--4 sentences, past simple). If they mix tenses, say: "Wait --- you're telling the story now. Past simple. 'I went.' 'I saw.' 'It happened.'"

**Last 5 min:** Say: "Who won? Who still has fingers up? That person --- tell us something surprising you HAVE done that nobody asked about."

> This game naturally forces the distinction between present perfect (the experience) and past simple (the details). Don't explain this --- let them feel it. Write errors on your error paper.

### 00:35--00:55 | BOOK: MODULE 9 GUIDE 1 --- SIMPLE PAST VS PRESENT PERFECT (20 min)

Say: "Open your book to Module 9, Guide 1 --- Simple Past vs Present Perfect. Read the explanation and ALL examples with your partner. You have 8 minutes. Read everything --- the examples, the notes, the explanations. Don't skip anything."

**First 8 min:** They read in pairs. You don't speak. Walk around checking they're reading, not chatting.

**Last 12 min:** Sit with each pair. THEY explain to you. Ask them:

- "When do we use past simple?" (Expected: Completed actions, usually with a specific time. Yesterday, in 2019, last week, when I was 10.)
- "When do we use present perfect?" (Expected: Unspecified time, incomplete events, open time periods, recent events.)
- "Give me an example of each." (Expected: "I went to France in 2019." / "I have been to France.")
- "If I ask 'Have you written to your mother?' and you say yes --- what tense do you answer in?" (Expected: Past simple --- "Yes, I wrote to her yesterday.")
- "Why does the answer switch to past simple?" (Expected: Because now you're giving a specific detail --- when it happened.)
- "Can you say 'I have gone to France in 2019'?" (No --- specific time = past simple.)

If they get confused, say: "Listen. HAVE YOU BEEN to France? --- no time, just the experience. I WENT to France IN 2019 --- specific time, finished action." They repeat both sentences.

**After checking all pairs, give these 6 situations. Say each one aloud:**

1. "Someone asks if you know how to cook. You learned in 2018." (Expected: "I have cooked many times." or "I learned to cook in 2018.")
2. "Your friend asks about your travel experience." (Expected: "I have been to 5 countries." NOT "I have been to 5 countries last year.")
3. "You want to talk about something you did last night." (Expected: Past simple --- "I watched a movie last night.")
4. "You want to say you've recently finished a project." (Expected: "I have just finished a project." or "I have finished it.")
5. "Tell me about a concert --- when was it?" (Expected: "I went to a concert in March.")
6. "Tell me about concerts in general --- your whole life." (Expected: "I have been to many concerts.")

One situation per student. If they freeze, say: "Think --- is there a specific time? Past simple. No specific time? Present perfect. Try again."

### 01:10--01:25 | PRODUCTION: INTERVIEW ABOUT LIFE EXPERIENCES (15 min)

Say: "You're going to interview your partner about their life experiences. But there's a rule: the FIRST question uses present perfect. The FOLLOW-UP questions use past simple. Watch."

**Write on the board:**

```
PATTERN:
Q: "Have you ever [past participle]...?"
A: "Yes, I have." / "No, I haven't."
Q: "When did you [base form]...?" / "Where did you [base form]...?"

```

**Demo with a student (2 min).** Say: "Watch. I'll interview [student name]."

- If yes: "When did you break it? How did it happen? What did the doctor say?"

- Are they switching tenses correctly? Present perfect for the experience, past simple for the follow-up?
- Common error: "Have you ever went?" --- say: "No. Have you ever GONE?"

### 01:45--02:05 | BOOK: MODULE 9 GUIDE 2 --- PAST PERFECT (20 min)

Say: "Open your book to Guide 2 --- Past Perfect. Read the explanation and examples with your partner. You have 8 minutes."

**First 8 min:** They read in pairs. Walk around silently.

**Last 12 min:** Sit with each pair. THEY explain to you. Ask them:

- "How do we form the past perfect?" (Expected: HAD + past participle.)
- "Why do we use it?" (Expected: To show which event happened FIRST when two things happened in the past.)
- "Give me an example." (Expected: "When we moved to Colombia, I had learned Spanish." --- First: learned Spanish. Then: moved to Colombia.)
- "Give me another one." (Expected: "I went to London after I had studied English." --- First: studied English. Then: went to London.)
- "What is the difference between 'I ate lunch' and 'I had eaten lunch when she arrived'?" (Expected: The second one shows the eating happened BEFORE she arrived.)
- "Can you give me a sentence with BEFORE or AFTER?" (Expected: "She had already left before I arrived." / "After he had finished his homework, he went out.")

If they struggle, say: "Think of it as TWO past events. Which one happened FIRST? That one gets HAD + past participle. The other one is simple past."

**Quick test --- say these situations and ask them to make sentences:**

1. "You studied all night. Then you took the exam." (Expected: "I had studied all night before I took the exam.")
2. "She left the party. Then he arrived." (Expected: "She had left the party when he arrived." / "She had already left before he arrived.")
3. "They ate all the food. We got to the restaurant." (Expected: "They had eaten all the food by the time we got to the restaurant.")
4. "I saved money for 2 years. Then I bought a car." (Expected: "I had saved money for 2 years before I bought a car.")
5. "He lost his phone. Then he realized it." (Expected: "He had lost his phone before he realized it.")
6. "We finished the project. The boss called." (Expected: "We had finished the project when the boss called.")

One per student. If they freeze, say: "Start with 'I had...' and then add what happened next."

### 02:05--02:20 | BOOK: MODULE 9 GUIDE 3 --- FUTURE PERFECT (15 min)

Say: "Open your book to Guide 3 --- Future Perfect. Read the explanation and examples. You have 6 minutes."

**First 6 min:** They read in pairs. Walk around silently.

**Last 9 min:** Sit with each pair. THEY explain to you. Ask them:

- "How do we form the future perfect?" (Expected: WILL HAVE + past participle.)
- "When do we use it?" (Expected: To talk about something that will be completed BEFORE a specific point in the future.)
- "Give me an example." (Expected: "By next year, I will have finished my degree.")
- "What about the future conditional --- how is it different?" (Expected: WOULD HAVE + past participle. It talks about something that would have happened under a different condition. "I would have finished if I had had more time.")
- "Give me a sentence with BY." (Expected: "By December, I will have saved enough money." / "By the time you arrive, I will have cooked dinner.")

**Quick test --- say these situations:**

1. "You're graduating next June. Talk about that." (Expected: "By next June, I will have graduated.")
2. "Your friend is arriving at 8. Dinner will be ready at 7." (Expected: "By the time you arrive, I will have finished cooking.")
3. "Your company reaches 10 years next month." (Expected: "By next month, my company will have been open for 10 years.")
4. "Imagine: you didn't study. What would have happened with the exam?" (Expected: "I would have failed the exam.")
5. "Imagine: you had more time yesterday. What about the report?" (Expected: "I would have finished the report.")
6. "What will you have accomplished by the end of this year?" (Open answer --- must use "will have + past participle.")

One per student. If they freeze, say: "Start with 'By [time], I will have...' and finish the thought."

### 02:40--03:00 | CORRECT THE TEACHER: STORY WITH 12 ERRORS (20 min)

Say: "Now I'm going to tell you a story. This story has 12 errors --- all related to perfect tenses. Present perfect, past perfect, AND future perfect. When you hear an error, shout 'WRONG!' and tell me the correct version. I'll keep score on the board. Ready?"

**Write on the board:** Student names in a row. Add a tally mark each time someone catches an error.

Read this story SLOWLY. Pause slightly before each error to give them time to catch it. Errors are marked for YOU --- do NOT read the corrections aloud:

---

"Let me tell you about my friend Carolina. Carolina is someone who has did everything in life. **(1 --- has DONE everything --- irregular past participle)**

She has traveled to 30 countries. Last year, she has gone to Japan. **(2 --- she WENT to Japan --- specific time = past simple, not present perfect)**

Before she went to Japan, she learned Japanese for two years. **(3 --- she HAD learned Japanese --- past perfect, because the learning happened BEFORE the trip)**

When she arrived in Tokyo, she realized she had forget her passport at the hotel. **(4 --- had FORGOTTEN --- irregular past participle)**

She told me: 'I have never saw anything so beautiful as Mount Fuji.' **(5 --- have never SEEN --- irregular past participle)**

She also said that she had ate sushi every single day. **(6 --- had EATEN --- irregular past participle)**

By the time she came home, she has visited 15 cities. **(7 --- she HAD visited --- past perfect, not present perfect, because it happened before another past event)**

Now Carolina is planning her next trip. By next December, she will has saved enough money. **(8 --- will HAVE saved --- will have, not will has)**

She says that by 2027, she will have went to every continent. **(9 --- will have GONE --- irregular past participle)**

Her sister is jealous. She said: 'If I have had more money, I would have traveled too.' **(10 --- If I HAD had more money --- third conditional, not 'have had')**

Carolina told her: 'By the time you finish university, I will had visited 50 countries.' **(11 --- I will HAVE visited --- will have, not will had)**

But honestly, Carolina have already accomplished more than most people do in a lifetime." **(12 --- HAS already accomplished --- third person singular, not 'have')**

---

After reading: review any errors the class missed. Say the wrong version first, then the correct version. They repeat the correct version twice.

**Pairs (8 min):** Say: "Now YOU are the teacher. Partner A: tell a short story --- 1 to 2 minutes --- about someone's life using deliberate errors with perfect tenses. Wrong past participles, wrong tense choice, wrong auxiliaries. Partner B: catch and correct. After 4 minutes, SWITCH."

Walk around. Write down good corrections AND missed errors on your error paper.

**Write on the board:**

```
TIMELINE OF MY LIFE

PAST (Past Perfect --- what happened first):

PRESENT (Present Perfect --- ongoing, up to now):

"By next year, I will have finished my degree."
"By the time I'm 40, I will have started my own business."
```

**Presentation (10 min):** Say: "Stand up. You're going to share your timeline with the class. Start from the past, move to present, end with future. Like telling the story of your life in 1 minute."

If the class spots an error, the presenting student corrects it on the spot. If they can't, you say the correct version. They repeat.

**Last 3 min:** Say: "Whose future perfect sentence was the most ambitious? Whose past perfect story was the most surprising?" Quick class discussion.

> Error paper. This activity tests whether they can use all three perfect tenses in context. If someone can only produce present perfect but struggles with past and future, note it.
