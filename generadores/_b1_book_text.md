Guide 1 – Question Tags 
 
In Spanish, we often finish a statement with a question when we already know the answer or 
when we are confirming. For example: 
“Tú eres Emile, ¿verdad? 
 
In English, we call these “question tags”, and they have a very simple pattern. The question 
tag uses the same auxiliary as the principal statement. 
If the statement is affirmative, the tag is negative. 
E.g. “We will go tomorrow, won’t* we?” 
 
If the statement is negative, the tag is affirmative. 
E.g. “We won’t go tomorrow, will we?” 
*Note: The question tag almost always uses the contraction (E.g.: will not → won’t). 
For “to be”: 
 “You are sick, aren’t you?” 
 “You aren’t sick, are you?” 
I am very tall, aren’t I? (“Amn’t” does not exist, so we use “aren’t” for “I”) 
For the continuous: 
“Sam is playing soccer, isn’t he?” 
“Sam isn’t playing soccer, is he?” 
Also, for the perfect: 
“Sam has played soccer, hasn’t he?” 
“Sam hasn’t played soccer, has he?” 
Sometimes, we omit the auxiliary “do/does/did” in the affirmative. But we use it in the tag. 
“Sam plays soccer, doesn’t he?” 
“You play soccer, don’t you?” 
“Andres played soccer, didn’t he?”

Guide 1 – “Either…or” 
 
We use “either” to say “o” or “cualquier”. It indicates TWO options. We often use “either…or” 
in a question or an affirmative sentence. 
1. Before a noun: 
“Podemos hablar o español o ingles.” → “We can speak either Spanish or English.” 
“Yo prefiero o carne o pollo.” → “I prefer either meat or chicken.” 
 
2. Before a verb: 
“Podemos o nadar o volar.” → “We can either swim or fly.” 
“Deberíamos o comer o tomar.” → We should either eat or drink.” 
 
3. To indicate indifference between 2 options. Similar to Spanish, we can say “of” after 
“either”. 
“Cualquier de los chicos le puede ayudar.” → “Either of the boys can help you.” 
“Cualquier de ellos le puede ayudar.” → “Either of them can help you.” (Note the object pronoun.) 
 
4. Finally, we can use “either” in a negative sentence to say “tampoco”. 
“John doesn’t have a car. I don’t have a car, either.” 
“John can’t play soccer, and I can’t, either.”

Guide 2 – “Neither…nor” 
 
“Neither…nor” (“ni…ni”) is the negative version of “either…or” in Spanish. It is also used to 
indicate 2 options. 
1. Before a noun: 
“No puedo hablar ni español ni ingles.” → “I can speak neither Spanish nor English.” 
 
IMPORTANT: “Neither…nor” are never used with a negative “not”. 
“I can’t speak neither Spanish nor English.” INCORRECT 
“I can speak neither Spanish nor English.” CORRECT 
 
2. Before a verb: 
“No podemos ni nadar ni volar.” → “We can neither swim nor fly.” 
“No deberíamos ni comer ni tomar.” → We should neither eat nor drink.” 
 
3. To indicate 0 of 2 options. Similar to Spanish, we can say “of” after “neither”. 
“Ninguno de los chicos le puede ayudar.” → “Neither of the boys can help you.” 
“Ninguno de ellos le puede ayudar.” → “Neither of them can help you.” (Note the object pronoun.) 
 
Finally, we can use “neither” in a negative sentence to say “tampoco”. Important: Notice that 
this expression uses an irregular form. 
“John doesn’t have a car. Neither do I.” 
“John can’t play soccer, and neither do I.”

Guide 3 – “Both…and” 
 
“Both…and” is the equivalent to “ambos…y” in Spanish, and is used in affirmative sentences. 
1. Before a noun: 
“Podemos hablar ambos español e ingles.” → “We can speak both Spanish and English.” 
 
2. Before a verb: 
“Podemos ambos nadar y volar.” → “We can both swim and fly.” 
“Deberíamos ambos comer y tomar.” → We should both eat and drink.” 
 
3. To indicate 2 of 2 options. Similar to Spanish, we can say “of” after “both”. 
“Ambos de los chicos le puede ayudar.” → “Both of the boys can help you.” 
“Ambos de ellos le puede ayudar.” → “Both of them can help you.” (Note the object pronoun.) 
 
Remember, we do not use “both” to say “tambien”; we use “also” or “too”. 
“John has a car.” → “I also have a car.” → “I have a car, too.” 
“John can play soccer, and I can, too.”

Guide 1 – Verbs of Deduction 
 
Similar to Spanish, we use verbs of deduction to express a supposition (something we think or 
believe). The verb depends on the certainty the speaker feels: 
Certainty 
Verb of Deduction 
English Example 
Spanish Translation 
Very certain 
Must 
He must be sick. 
Él debe estar enfermo. 
 
 
 
 
Partially certain 
Might 
He might be sick. 
Él podría estar enfermo. 
May 
He may be sick. 
Could 
He could be sick. 
 
 
 
 
Very certain of 
the opposite 
Can’t 
He can’t be sick. 
Él no puede estar enfermo. 
Must not (mustn’t) 
He mustn’t be sick. 
 
It is very common to use deduction with the verb “to be”. We often use it in the continuous 
and perfect tenses. 
“He must be playing soccer.” 
“He must have arrived already.”

Guide 1 – The Third Conditional (Straight) 
 
The 3rd conditional (straight) refers to something that did (or did not) happen in the past, and 
therefore, a certain condition did (or did not) exist in the past. 
It has the form: 
Clausula con “si” 
HUBIER- -A, -AS, -AMOS, -AN + PARTICIPLE 
Clausula Condicional 
HABR -ÍA, -ÍAS, -ÍAMOS, -ÍAN + PARTICIPLE 
Si yo hubiera estudiado … 
…habría aprobado el examen. 
 
Clause with “if” 
HAD + PARTICIPLE 
Conditional clause 
WOULD HAVE + PARTICIPLE 
If I had studied… 
…I would have passed the exam. 
 
Therefore, I did not study (in the past) and consequently I did not pass the exam (in the past). 
The probability of studying is now 0%, because past events cannot be changed.

Guide 2 – The Third Conditional (Mixed) 
 
The 3rd conditional (mixed) is very similar to the 3rd conditional (straight). But, it refers to something 
that did (or did not) happen in the past, and therefore, a certain condition does (or does not) exist in 
the present/future. 
It has the form: 
Clausula con “si” 
HUBIER- -A, -AS, -AMOS, -AN + PARTICIPLE 
Clausula Condicional 
VERB + -ÍA, -ÍAS, -ÍAMOS, -ÍAN 
Si yo hubiera estudiado … 
…yo trabajaría en Roma hoy. 
 
Clause with “if” 
HAD + PARTICIPLE 
Conditional clause 
WOULD HAVE + PARTICIPLE 
If I had studied… 
…I would work in Rome today. 
 
Therefore, I did not study (in the past), and consequently, I do not live in Rome today. 
The probability of studying is now 0%, because past events cannot be changed. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
In the 3rd conditional (straight), I did 
not study in the past and 
consequently did not live in Rome in 
the PAST. 
In the 3rd conditional (mixed), I did 
not study in the past and 
consequently did not live in Rome in 
the PRESENT.

Guide 2 – Expressing Regret 
 
 “Should/could/would have” are sometimes used to express regrets. 
I should have gone to the game. Everyone had a great time! 
Two other expressions are commonly used to express regret: 
1. “To wish”. Similar to “ojalá” in Spanish.  
I wish I had gone to the game. → Ojalá hubiera ido al partido. 
2. “If only”. More common in England. In North America, it is considered a bit dramatic. 
If only I had gone to the game. → Si tan solo hubiera ido al juego. 
 
Note that both expressions use the “had + participle” form similar to the 3rd conditional. 
 
North American speakers often use both expressions with “would have + participle”. This is incorrect, 
but generally accepted English. 
I wish I would have gone to the game.

Guide 1 – Simple Relative Pronouns

Guide 2 – Restrictive Relative Clauses

Guide 3 – Non-Restrictive Relative Clauses

Guide 4 – Omitting Relative Pronouns 
 
10. When the relative pronoun is used as the object of a clause, it can be omitted optionally. 
“My mom is a person who I love.” → My mom is a person I love. 
 
 
11. When the relative pronoun is used as the subject of a clause, it cannot be omitted. 
“My mom is a person who loves me.” → My mom is a person who loves me. 
 
 
12. The same rule applies for “which” and “that”, “when”, etc. 
“A Ferrari is a car that I drive on the weekend.” → “A Ferrari is a car I drive on the weekend.” 
“A Ferrari is a car that has a big engine.” 
13. Note: Even when the relative pronoun is optional, we often include it in the sentence for 
clarity. Written or academic work will usually include the pronoun.

2. “Embedded” = “encapsulado” indicates the question is encapsulated in a longer phrase.

3. Important! The relative clause uses the AFFIRMATIVE ORGANIZATION, not the question. 
CORRECT: Can you tell me what you need? 
INCORRECT: Can you tell me what do you need?

Question forms: 
Do you know…? (Sentence ends with “?”)

Guide 1 – Order of Objects 
 
1. Many sentences include both direct and indirect objects. 
2. The direct object is the person/thing that receives the action of the verb directly. 
I buy a flower. 
I give a flower. 
3. The indirect object is the person/thing affected by the action of the verb, but not directly. 
I buy a flower for Anita. 
I give a flower to Anita. 
 
4. I buy/give a flower (direct). I do not buy/give Anita (indirect). Anita is affected by the action. 
5. English permits either the direct or indirect object to be positioned first. 
a. Case 1: [direct] + “to/for” + [indirect] – the preposition “to” / “for” must be included. 
I buy a flower for Anita. 
I give a flower to Anita. 
b. Case 2: [indirect] + [direct] – no preposition between the objects. 
I buy Anita a flower. 
I give Anita a flower.

Guide 2 – Say, Tell, Ask 
 
1. Say = “Decir” 
a. Usually, “say” refers to exact words, which form the direct object. 
I say hello. 
b. We can add an indirect object, but it is optional. 
I say hello to Janel. 
c. Phrases with “say” are therefore always organized [direct] + “to” + [indirect]. 
 
2. Tell = contar/informar 
a. Usually, “tell” refers to information communicated to a person (the indirect object). 
I tell my mom. 
b. We can add a direct object, but it is optional. 
I tell my mom the time. 
c. Phrases with “tell” are therefore always organized [indirect] [direct]. 
 
3. “Ask” = Preguntar. It functions exactly as for “tell”. 
I ask my mom. (Indirect object is required.) 
I ask my mom the time. (Direct object is optional.) 
 
4. “Say”, “tell”, and “ask” can be used with a relative pronoun – exactly as in Spanish. 
Daniella said that she was happy. 
Daniella told me where she lives. 
Daniella asked me if I was sick.

Guide 1 – Simple Past vs Present Perfect 
 
Simple Past: We use the simple past tense to talk about actions that are completed or finished in the 
past – usually at a specific time. 
I went to the store yesterday. 
Present perfect: We use the present perfect for: 
1. Events that occurred in an unknown or unspecified period of time. (Or did not happen.) 
a. For example: “I have visited England.” We do not know (or say) when. Only, that the 
event has happened in the past. 
b. “I haven’t visited England.” (The event did not happen at any point in time.) 
c. “Have you ever* tried to eat fish?” *The adverb of frequency “ever” emphasises “aún una vez”. 
2. Events that are not complete. 
a. “I have lived in Bucaramanga since 2014.” (I started living in Bucaramanga in 2014, and 
am still living in Bucaramanga today.) 
3. Events that occurred in a period of time that is open. 
a. “I have already worked three hours today.” (Today is not finished.) 
b. “I haven’t worked yet today.” 
4. Happened very recently. 
a. “Okay, I’ve (just)* finished my homework. Now what should we do?” *The word adverb of 
frequency “just” in this context means “apenas” or “Acabo de terminar mis tareas…” 
We often ask a question in the present perfect and answer it in the simple past with an exact time. 
 
Question: Have you written to your mother? 
Answer: Yes, I wrote to her yesterday.

Guide 2 – The Past Perfect 
 
1. The present perfect tense is actually a form of the past tense. The name “present perfect” 
refers to the auxiliary “to have” being used in the present tense. 
2. The past perfect tense uses the same participle, but the auxiliary verb “to have” is in the past 
tense (“had”). The negative form often uses the contraction (“had not” → “hadn’t”). 
“I have visited” → “I had visited” 
“I had visited.” → “Había visitado” 
3. We use the past perfect together with the simple past to talk about events that happened 
before another past event. For example: 
“When we moved to Colombia, I had learned Spanish.” 
“Cuando nos mudamos a Colombia, ya había aprendido español.” 
 
4. Using the past perfect establishes the relationship between those two separate times. The 
past perfect is a “narrative tense” because it helps us explain the relationship in a story.

Guide 3 – The Future Perfect 
 
1. In this guide, we will learn two simple extensions: the future and conditional perfect. 
Remember, with the perfect tenses, we simply use the appropriate tense of “to have” + the 
past participle. 
Tense 
Form of “to have” 
Example 
Present perfect 
Have/has 
I have arrived at the airport. 
Past perfect 
Had 
I had arrived when you called. 
Future perfect 
Will have 
I will have arrived before 5:00. 
Future conditional 
perfect 
Would have 
I would have arrived before 5:00. 
 
 
I could/may/might have played… 
 
2. The future perfect describes an event that will be complete by a certain time limit. We often 
specify the time limit using a proposition as follows: 
 
I will have arrived by 5:00. → Habré llegado a las 5:00. (“By” is very common here.) 
I will have arrived before 5:00. → Habré llegado antes de las 5:00. 
I will have arrived at 5:00. → Habré llegado a las 5:00. 
I will have arrived in 3 hours. → Habré llegado en 3 horas. 
I will have arrived 3 hours from now. → Habré llegado dentro de 3 horas. 
I will have arrived when you finish the job. → Habré llegado cuando termine el trabajo. 
I will have arrived by the time you finish the job. → Habré llegado cuando termines el trabajo. 
 
3. Note that we can write the sentence in the reverse order, adding a comma: 
I will have arrived by tomorrow. → By tomorrow, I will have arrived. 
 
4. We usually avoid using “going to” with the future perfect, since we use “will” to make future 
predictions or commitments. 
I am going to have arrived by 5:00. (Understandable but unusual.) 
 
5. Finally, the negative and interrogative forms are exactly what we would expect. 
Question: Will you have finished the game by 8:30? 
Negative: We won’t have finished the game by 8:30.

Guide 4 – The Perfect Continous 
 
1. We use the present simple tense to express what we were doing at a certain time in the past.  
Tense 
Simple perfect 
Perfect continuous 
Past 
I had played. (Yo había jugado.) 
I had been playing. (Yo había estado jugando.) 
Present 
I have played. (Yo he jugado.) 
I have been playing. (Yo he estado jugando.) 
Future 
I will have played. (Yo habré jugado.) 
I will have been playing. (Yo habré estado jugando.) 
 
 
2. The past perfect continuous describes an event that started and ended in the past. 
When my parents arrived in Bucaramanga, I had been living here for 5 years. 
 
3. The present perfect continuous describes an event that was occurring at an unspecified 
previous time or is unfinished in the present: 
I have been living in Bucaramanga since 2014. 
 
4. The future perfect continuous describes an event that will be occurring at a certain future 
time. 
By 2019, I will have been living in Bucaramanga for* 6 years. 
 
5. It is very common to use “for” (similar to “durante”) in this context, because we often use the 
future perfect continuous to talk about a predicted duration of time.

Guide 5 – Already and Yet 
 
 
1. These two words are commonly used with perfect tenses. 
 
2. Already: Indicates that the verb occurred earlier than the time referenced by the sentence. It 
is positioned as an adverb of frequency. Logically, it is usually used in affirmative sentences. 
 
Present perfect: I have already played soccer. 
Past perfect: When you called, I had already played soccer. 
Future perfect: When you call, I will have already played soccer. 
 
3. Yet: Indicates that the event has not occurred at the time referenced by the sentence (but 
might occur later). It is used only in negative sentences and questions, and comes at the end. 
 
Present perfect: I haven’t played soccer yet. 
Past perfect: When you called, I hadn’t played soccer yet. 
Future: When you call, I won’t have played soccer yet. 
 
4. We usually put the word “yet” at the end of the perfect clause. It is also possible (although less 
common) to do the reverse. In this case, we normally put the “yet” after the perfect clause, in 
the middle of the sentence. 
I hadn’t played soccer yet, when you called.

Guide 1 – The Active Causative 
 
We use the causative when we cause (causar) a person (or a thing) to do something. 
 
In Spanish, for example,  
Hago que Manuela trabaje. 
Le digo a Manuela que trabaje. 
 
Spanish uses the subjunctive mood for the causative. In English, we use the form: 
Subject 
Causative verb 
Person who acts 
Action (verb) 
Complement 
I 
Ask 
Manuela 
To work. 
N/A 
I 
Help 
Manuela 
work 
N/A 
 
Notice that the verb “ask” uses the action verb in the infinitive (it includes “to”) but “help” does not. 
Verbs that use the infinitive (“to”) 
Verbs that do not use the infinitive (no “to”) 
Ask = Pedir 
Help = Ayudar 
Allow = Permitir 
Let = Dejar / No Impedir / Permitir 
Get = Conseguir que 
Make = Obligar 
Tell = Ordenar 
Have = Obligar (Uncommon, formal) 
Want = Querer 
 
Need = Necesitar 
 
Most verbs use the infinitive. The rule is the same as for gerunds in Level A2. If the verb implies that 
the main action has not started, then we use the infinitive. If the verb implies that the main action has 
started, then we do not use the infinitive. 
For example, if I help someone do an action, then the action has started. If I want them to do it, the 
action might not have started. 
 
Note that the tense of the main verb does not affect the rest of the sentence (nor the negative): 
I want you to go. → I don’t want you to go. → I will want you to go. → I wanted you to go.
