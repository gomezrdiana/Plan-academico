### ☐ 01:05–01:40 | TBLT: BLIND ARTIST (35 min)
**How it works:** You give ONE student a paper with a scene written on it. Their partner CANNOT see it. The one with the paper describes the scene out loud, and the partner draws what they hear. They can talk back and forth — "Where is the house?" "On top of the hill." "How big?" — but the artist NEVER sees the original. At the end, they compare the drawing with the description and see how close it got. The student HAS to use advanced adjectives and correct order, otherwise the drawing won't match. That's the point: they NEED to communicate well to succeed.

**3 pairs. Write both scenes on separate papers BEFORE class.**

**Discover Scene 1 (3-4 min):** Show Scene 1 to the whole class. Students read it and point out words they DON'T understand. For each unknown word: ONE student looks it up on their phone, but CANNOT say the translation — they must explain it in English or with gestures so the others understand. Next word, a different student looks it up. Rotate.

**Round 1 (10 min):** Give Scene 1 to Student A in each pair. Student B draws. They talk, ask questions, describe details. Student A cannot show the paper.

**Discover Scene 2 (3-4 min):** Same process — show Scene 2, students discover unknown words in English. One phone at a time, explain in English only.

**Round 2 (10 min):** Give Scene 2 to Student B. Now Student A draws. Roles reversed.

**Compare & Vote (5 min):** All 6 students see every drawing next to the original description. Class votes: which drawing is the most accurate? Which pair communicated best?

**Scene 1 (print or write on a paper — give to Student A):**
"A magnificent old Victorian wooden house on top of a desolate grey hill. In front of the house there is a decrepit rusty metal fence. The sky is a breathtaking orange and purple sunset. A gaunt black cat sits on the intricate stone steps."

**Scene 2 (print or write on a paper — give to Student B):**
"A radiant young woman wearing an exquisite long red silk dress stands in front of a luminous round golden mirror. The room has meticulous small white marble tiles on the floor. Through the window you can see a menacing dark grey storm cloud."

### ☐ 02:27–02:42 | DOGME: HOT TOPIC (15 min)
**What is this?** There is no plan for this activity. YOU start a conversation and let it flow. Your only job is to make sure they use advanced adjectives while they talk. This is about REAL conversation — not exercises, not scripts. Just talking.

**How to do it:** Write ONE question on the board. Read it out loud. Then SHUT UP and let them talk. If the conversation dies, ask a follow-up. If someone says "big" or "nice" or "bad", stop them: "Don't say 'nice.' Use an advanced adjective. Which one fits?" They correct themselves. That's it.

**Pick ONE question from this list (whichever fits the group's energy):**
1. "Describe the most breathtaking place you've ever been to. Why?"
2. "What's the most decrepit building in Bucaramanga? What should they do with it?"
3. "Describe your dream house in detail — I want to SEE it."
4. "What's the most exquisite meal you've ever had? Describe everything."
5. "Describe a person you admire without saying their name. We guess."

> You don't control where the conversation goes. You only make sure they speak English and use advanced adjectives. If they go off topic but they're talking in English — LET THEM.

### ☐ 02:42–03:02 | DESIGN THINKING MINI (20 min)
**What is this?** Students solve a real problem in 5 steps. Everything they say must use advanced adjectives to describe the problem and the solution. It sounds complicated but it's just 5 simple questions answered one at a time.

**The problem (write on board):** "The park near your house is ugly and nobody uses it. Redesign it."

**Step by step — the teacher reads each step out loud, gives them time, then moves to the next:**

**Step 1 — UNDERSTAND (3 min):** "Describe the park right now. What does it look like? Use adjectives." They describe: "It's desolate, the benches are decrepit, the grass is..." You write key adjectives on the board as they say them.

**Step 2 — DEFINE (2 min):** "In ONE sentence: what is the main problem?" Each pair writes one sentence. Example: "The park is desolate because there's nothing for young people to do."

**Step 3 — IDEAS (5 min):** "In pairs: write 5 ideas to fix it. Use adjectives to describe what the park WILL look like." Example: "We could add an exquisite garden with intricate pathways..." No idea is bad. Write everything.

**Step 4 — PICK ONE (2 min):** Each pair picks their best idea.

**Step 5 — PRESENT (8 min):** Each pair presents their solution to the class (2 min each). They MUST describe the new park using at least 5 advanced adjectives. Class votes: which redesign would you visit?

> This is the first time doing Design Thinking. Keep it simple. Read each step, give them time, move on. Don't overthink it.

### ☐ 03:02–03:22 | LEGO DESCRIBE & BUILD (20 min)
**What is this?** Students build something using whatever is on their desk — pens, erasers, paper, books, phone cases, water bottles, anything. It doesn't need to look real. It's a metaphor. Then they describe it to the class using advanced adjectives. The point is: everyone builds, everyone speaks. No spectators.

**Build (5 min):** Say: "Build your dream vacation destination using the objects on your desk. It doesn't have to look real — it's a metaphor. A pen can be a mountain, an eraser can be a beach. Be creative. You have 5 minutes. Go."

**Describe (12 min):** Each student stands up and describes their creation to the class. They MUST use at least 5 advanced adjectives. Example: "This pen represents a breathtaking mountain. These erasers are the exquisite white sand beach. This crumpled paper is the desolate desert nearby..." Class asks questions: "Why is this part luminous?" "What's the intricate thing in the corner?"

**Vote (3 min):** Class votes most creative description.

> 6 students = 2 min each. Everyone participates.
