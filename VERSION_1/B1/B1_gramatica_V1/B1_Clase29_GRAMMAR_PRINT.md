## 6. BOOK M39 + BOARD RACE — 12 min — STAND UP

Write on the board (before class):

```
M39 — POSSESSIVE PRONOUNS AS OBJECTS (Book p.319)

Rule 1 (verbatim): "We use the question word WHOSE
   to ask about ownership: 'Whose car is that?'"

Rule 2: The pronouns work like in Spanish:
   ADJECTIVE  →  PRONOUN
   my         →  mine
   your       →  yours
   his        →  his      (SAME — careful!)
   her        →  hers
   our        →  ours
   their      →  theirs
   its        →  its      (rare as pronoun)

Rule 3: Apostrophe + s works as a pronoun too:
   Peter's dog → It's Peter's.
   My brother's dog → It's my brother's.

ADJECTIVE → before a NOUN ("my car")
PRONOUN → alone as OBJECT ("It's mine")
QUESTION WORD → WHOSE
```

Say it standing at the board: *"When the possessive goes BEFORE a noun, use the ADJECTIVE. When it STANDS ALONE as an answer or object, use the PRONOUN. Rule 1 from page 319: WHOSE for ownership. M40 doesn't exist — next class we jump straight to M41."*

## 7. WALKING TRANSFORMATION — 16 min — STAND UP

Two zones in the room (tape labels on opposite walls):
- **WRONG zone (left):** adjective in object position — "It is my" / "It's her" / "is our"
- **RIGHT zone (right):** pronoun in object position — "It's mine" / "It's hers" / "is ours"

Hand each student **ONE Set A subject card** (yo / tú / él / ella / nos / ellos — if >6 students, duplicate) **+ ONE Set D slip card** with a wrong-shape sentence. Each student keeps both cards in hand through Round 1 and Round 2.

### Round 1 — Walk it — 8 min

Each student:
1. Stands in WRONG zone, reads slip aloud with the wrong form.
2. Walks to RIGHT zone shouting the corrected form (and raises subject card — "yo" → MINE, "ella" → HERS, "nos" → OURS, etc.).
3. Cohort confirms with thumbs + names the pair aloud: *"my → mine, correct."*

Suggested slips:

```
"It is my."                          → "It's mine."          (yo)
"Is this Anne's dog? Yes, it's her." → "Yes, it's hers."     (ella)
"The printer is our."                → "The printer is ours."(nos)
"Whose is this car? Is the my."      → "It's mine."          (yo)
"Are those Frank's keys? They are my."→ "They're mine."      (yo)
"This is the waitress' shoe. It's her." → "It's hers."       (ella)
"That is the teacher's car. It's his."→ "It's his."          (TRAP — same shape, do not change)
"Is that their bike? It's their."    → "It's theirs."        (ellos)
```

> The "his → his" trap is intentional — temperance: do not invent a change where none exists.

### Round 2 — Reverse — 4 min

Swap slips. Each student starts in RIGHT, walks to WRONG saying out loud WHY it's wrong: *"'It is my' is wrong — 'my' is an adjective, it goes before a noun ('my car'). When alone as object, English uses MINE: 'It's mine.' Book page 319."*

### Close — 2 min

2 volunteers walk their transformation in front of the group. Cohort counts correct pairs. Note errors with NAMES in your personal notebook.

### Sorting — 2 zones — 4 min — STAND UP

Use the two floor labels already taped (from Block 7): **ADJECTIVE — before a NOUN** vs **PRONOUN — alone, as OBJECT**.

**Shuffle Set B + Set C together (14 cards)** and hand each student 2–3 cards at random. Each student walks every card they hold to the right zone, one at a time, saying it aloud: *"My — adjective — goes before a noun, 'my car'."* / *"Mine — pronoun — stands alone, 'It's mine.'"*

Trap: *"his"* and *"its"* appear in BOTH sets — they can be either adjective or pronoun. The student decides which zone, explains why. Discuss briefly with the group.

Then in groups of 2: build one phrase per zone:
- ADJ "her" + NOUN: "her wallet"
- PRON "hers" alone: "The wallet is hers."

## 9. HUMAN LINE-UP + 3 STATIONS — 12 min — STAND UP

### Human line-up — 4 min

**Use Set E sentence builder cards.** Each student gets ONE card (subject, verb, pronoun, "Whose", or "?"). You call out a target sentence; whoever holds a relevant card walks to the front and stands in the correct word-order to form it. Cohort verifies the order.

Targets to call (3–4 rounds, swap cards between rounds):
- *"It is mine."* → 3 students with cards "It" + "is" + "mine"
- *"The keys are hers."* → 4 students: "The keys" + "are" + "hers"
- *"Whose car is that?"* → 5 students: "Whose" + "car" + "is" + "that" + "?"
- *"Whose keys are those?"* → 5 students: "Whose" + "keys" + "are" + "those" + "?"

After each round: cohort reads the line aloud. Reshuffle cards. Next round.

### 3 stations — 8 min — bell every ~2.5 min

```
STATION A — WHOSE QUESTIONS
Build 4 "Whose" questions about your workplace +
answer each with a pronoun:
"Whose is this laptop? — It's mine."
"Whose are those keys? — They're hers."
"Whose is the meeting room on the 2nd floor? — It's ours on Mondays."
"Whose is this jacket? — It's his — the engineer's."

STATION B — MINE/YOURS/OURS STATEMENTS
Build 4 statements with the pronoun as object (never
the adjective in object position):
"The printer is mine."
"The coffee machine is ours."
"Those keys are hers."
"This wallet is not mine — it's his."

STATION C — COMPARATIVES with PRONOUNS
Replace the second adjective+noun with a pronoun
(book example: "My hair is longer than your hair." →
"My hair is longer than yours."):
"My printer is faster than your printer." → "...than yours."
"Your office is bigger than her office." → "...than hers."
"Their meeting room is newer than our meeting room." → "...than ours."
```

You circulate, correct softly (especially adjective in object position), note errors with NAMES.

K: "Whose is this coffee mug?"    L: "It's yours."     → "Confirmed!"

### Speed drill — 6 min — circle

You say ADJECTIVE+NOUN; the next student replaces with the PRONOUN in a full sentence:

```
"my car"          → "It's mine."
"your house"      → "It's yours."
"her keys"        → "They're hers."
"his wallet"      → "It's his."        (same shape!)
"our printer"     → "It's ours."
"their backpacks" → "They're theirs."
"my book"         → "It's mine."
"her cat"         → "It's hers."
"our team"        → "It's ours."
"their office"    → "It's theirs."
```

Then chorus: you ask *"Whose is this X?"* and the cohort answers in chorus with an alternating pronoun.
